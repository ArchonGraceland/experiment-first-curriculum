# Experiment Guide — All 80 Modules

This document describes the proposed experiment for each module in detail: what the student does, what they observe, what makes it engaging, and what materials are needed. These are starting points — refine based on testing with the target learner.

---

## PHYSICS — CORE (P1–P14)

### P1: Forces and Motion
**Question:** Do heavier objects fall faster than light ones?
**What the student does:** Drop pairs of objects with different masses from the same height — a full water bottle and an empty one, a tennis ball and a basketball, a book and a sheet of paper. Time each with a phone stopwatch. Then crumple the paper into a tight ball and drop it again alongside the flat sheet.
**The surprise:** The masses don't matter — the crumpled paper and the book hit at nearly the same time. It's air resistance, not weight, that creates the difference. This directly contradicts the intuition that "heavy = fast."
**Key observation:** When drag is minimized, all objects accelerate at the same rate regardless of mass.
**Materials:** Various household objects of different masses, phone with stopwatch, measuring tape.
**Estimated cost:** $0 (household items)

---

### P2: Gravity and Free Fall
**Question:** Can you measure the strength of gravity using coffee filters?
**What the student does:** Drop stacks of 1, 2, 4, and 8 nested coffee filters from the same height. Time each fall. The single filter flutters slowly; the stack of 8 drops much faster. Plot number of filters vs. fall time. Then calculate the approximate terminal velocity for each stack.
**The surprise:** Doubling the mass doesn't halve the fall time — the relationship isn't linear. This introduces the idea that drag force depends on velocity, not just shape.
**Key observation:** Terminal velocity increases with mass because a heavier object must fall faster before drag equals gravitational force.
**Materials:** 20+ flat-bottom coffee filters, stopwatch, measuring tape, step stool or stairwell for height.
**Estimated cost:** $3

---

### P3: Energy Conservation
**Question:** Does a ball rolling down a ramp always reach the same speed at the bottom, regardless of the ramp's shape?
**What the student does:** Build ramps of different steepness using books and a rigid board. Release a marble from the same height each time but on ramps with different angles. Measure the speed at the bottom by timing how long it takes to cross a measured distance on a flat surface.
**The surprise:** The speed at the bottom is the same regardless of the ramp angle — only the starting height matters. Steeper ramps get there faster, but the final speed is identical.
**Key observation:** Potential energy at the top converts entirely to kinetic energy at the bottom. The path doesn't matter, only the height.
**Materials:** Rigid board or track, books for elevation, marbles or small balls, ruler, stopwatch.
**Estimated cost:** $0–5

### P4: Electricity Fundamentals
**Question:** What happens to brightness when you add more light bulbs to a circuit?
**What the student does:** Build a simple circuit with a battery, wires, and one LED. Measure the brightness (visually and with a phone light meter app). Add a second LED in series — both get dimmer. Then rewire them in parallel — both are bright again. Measure voltage across each LED with a cheap multimeter.
**The surprise:** In series, adding more bulbs makes everything dimmer. In parallel, each bulb stays bright. This is counterintuitive — "more bulbs" doesn't mean "more light" unless you wire it right.
**Key observation:** In series circuits, resistance adds up and current decreases. In parallel circuits, each branch gets the full voltage.
**Materials:** 9V battery, alligator clip wires, 3–4 LEDs, 220Ω resistors, cheap multimeter.
**Estimated cost:** $12–15

### P5: Static Electricity and Charge
**Question:** Can you bend water with a balloon?
**What the student does:** Charge a balloon by rubbing it on wool or hair. Hold it near a thin stream of water from a faucet — the stream bends toward the balloon. Then build a simple electroscope from a jar, a paperclip, and aluminum foil strips. Charge objects and test what makes the foil leaves spread apart.
**The surprise:** The water bends even though nothing is touching it. The balloon exerts force through empty space. The electroscope leaves fly apart when charge is transferred but slowly fall back as charge leaks away.
**Key observation:** Charged objects can attract neutral objects through induction. Like charges repel; opposite charges attract.
**Materials:** Balloons, wool cloth, glass jar, paper clip, aluminum foil, comb, faucet.
**Estimated cost:** $0–3

### P6: Magnetism
**Question:** Can you build a magnet from a battery and a nail?
**What the student does:** Wrap insulated copper wire around an iron nail (50+ turns). Connect the ends to a battery. Test how many paperclips the electromagnet picks up. Vary the number of coil turns and the number of batteries. Then map the magnetic field around a bar magnet using iron filings on paper.
**The surprise:** More coils = stronger magnet. More current = stronger magnet. But the instant you disconnect the battery, the magnetism (mostly) disappears. The nail was only a temporary magnet.
**Key observation:** Electric current creates a magnetic field. The field strength depends on current and number of coils. Electromagnets are controllable magnets.
**Materials:** Iron nail, insulated copper wire (22 gauge), D batteries, paperclips, iron filings, bar magnet, paper.
**Estimated cost:** $8–10

### P7: Waves and Sound
**Question:** Why does a guitar string change pitch when you shorten it?
**What the student does:** Stretch a rubber band or string between two fixed points. Pluck it and note the pitch. Place a pencil underneath to shorten the vibrating length — the pitch goes up. Measure the length at several points and use a phone tuner app to record the frequency. Plot length vs. frequency.
**The surprise:** Halving the length doubles the frequency — it's an inverse relationship. This is why guitar frets are spaced the way they are.
**Key observation:** Frequency is inversely proportional to the length of the vibrating medium. Shorter string = higher pitch = higher frequency.
**Materials:** Rubber bands or string, pencil, ruler, phone with tuner/frequency analyzer app, two fixed posts (or a box).
**Estimated cost:** $0–3

### P8: Light and Optics
**Question:** Why do things look bent in water?
**What the student does:** Place a pencil in a glass of water and observe how it appears to bend at the surface. Then shine a flashlight beam through water, glass, and air at different angles and trace the light path. Use a prism to split white light into a full spectrum. Measure the angle of refraction for different materials.
**The surprise:** Light actually changes speed when it enters a denser medium, and that speed change is what bends it. Different colors (wavelengths) bend at slightly different angles — that's why a prism makes a rainbow.
**Key observation:** Refraction occurs because light travels at different speeds in different materials. The angle of bending depends on the material's refractive index.
**Materials:** Glass prism, flashlight, glass of water, protractor, white paper, laser pointer (optional).
**Estimated cost:** $8–12

### P9: The Electromagnetic Spectrum
**Question:** Is there light you can't see?
**What the student does:** Use a prism to project a visible spectrum on white paper. Place a thermometer just beyond the red end of the spectrum where no visible light appears — the temperature rises. That's infrared radiation. Then take UV-sensitive beads outside and watch them change color in sunlight. Test what blocks UV: sunscreen, glass, plastic, sunglasses.
**The surprise:** The thermometer detects "invisible light" beyond red. The UV beads prove there's also invisible light beyond violet. Visible light is just a tiny slice of what's out there.
**Key observation:** The electromagnetic spectrum extends far beyond visible light in both directions. Different wavelengths carry different energies and interact with matter differently.
**Materials:** Glass prism, thermometer, UV-sensitive beads, sunscreen, various materials to test UV blocking, flashlight.
**Estimated cost:** $8–10

### P10: Heat and Thermodynamics
**Question:** Why does a metal spoon feel colder than a wooden spoon at the same temperature?
**What the student does:** Place a metal spoon, a wooden spoon, and a plastic spoon in the same bowl of hot water for 5 minutes. They're all the same temperature now. Touch them — the metal one feels much hotter. Then reverse the experiment: put them all in ice water. The metal one feels coldest. Measure actual temperatures with a thermometer to confirm they're identical.
**The surprise:** They're all the same temperature, but they feel different. The sensation of "hot" or "cold" is about heat transfer rate (thermal conductivity), not actual temperature.
**Key observation:** Materials with high thermal conductivity transfer heat to/from your hand faster, creating a stronger temperature sensation even though the actual temperature is the same.
**Materials:** Metal spoon, wooden spoon, plastic spoon, bowls, hot water, ice water, thermometer.
**Estimated cost:** $0–3

### P11: Momentum and Collisions
**Question:** What happens when a moving marble hits a stationary one?
**What the student does:** Set up a flat track (a ruler with edges, or a grooved piece of cardboard). Roll one marble into a stationary marble and observe: the moving marble stops, and the stationary one moves at nearly the same speed. Then try different mass combinations — a large marble hitting a small one, a small one hitting a large one. Measure distances traveled to compare velocities.
**The surprise:** The first marble stops dead when it hits an equal-mass target. Momentum transfers almost perfectly. But when masses are unequal, both marbles move — and the total momentum is conserved.
**Key observation:** In collisions, total momentum before = total momentum after. This is the law of conservation of momentum.
**Materials:** Marbles of different sizes, flat track or ruler groove, measuring tape, stopwatch.
**Estimated cost:** $3–5

---

### P12: Fluid Dynamics and Pressure
**Question:** How can a heavy steel ship float when a steel ball sinks?
**What the student does:** Drop a ball of modeling clay into water — it sinks. Then reshape the same clay into a boat shape — it floats. Measure the weight of water displaced by the boat vs. the ball. Then build a Cartesian diver from a plastic bottle, a pen cap, and a small piece of modeling clay — squeeze the bottle and the diver sinks; release and it floats.
**The surprise:** The clay weighs the same both times. Floating vs. sinking has nothing to do with what something is made of — it's about whether the object displaces enough water to equal its own weight.
**Key observation:** An object floats when it displaces a volume of water whose weight equals the object's weight. Shape determines how much water is displaced.
**Materials:** Modeling clay, container of water, plastic bottle, pen cap, scale (optional).
**Estimated cost:** $0–3

### P13: Projectile Motion
**Question:** At what angle should you launch something to make it go the farthest?
**What the student does:** Build a simple launcher (a spoon catapult, or a rubber band launcher on a protractor base). Launch a small ball at 15°, 30°, 45°, 60°, and 75°. Mark where it lands each time. Measure the range. Do three trials per angle to account for variation.
**The surprise:** 45° gives the maximum range — not straight up, not flat. And 30° and 60° give the same range as each other, as do 15° and 75°. Complementary angles are symmetric.
**Key observation:** Projectile motion is the combination of horizontal (constant) velocity and vertical (accelerating) velocity. The optimal launch angle for maximum range is 45°.
**Materials:** Spoon or ruler catapult, rubber bands, small balls, protractor, measuring tape.
**Estimated cost:** $0–5

### P14: Simple Machines and Work
**Question:** Can you lift something heavy using only one finger?
**What the student does:** Try to lift a heavy book with one finger directly — hard. Then set up a lever (a ruler on a pencil fulcrum) and lift the same book by pressing down on the other end — easy. Measure the force needed at different fulcrum positions. Then set up a pulley system with rope and a broom handle — show how adding pulleys reduces the force needed but increases the distance you have to pull.
**The surprise:** You can lift something much heavier than you can normally manage. But you pull the rope a much longer distance than the object rises. The total work is the same — you just trade force for distance.
**Key observation:** Simple machines don't reduce work; they redirect or redistribute it. Mechanical advantage = the factor by which force is reduced, at the cost of increased distance.
**Materials:** Ruler, pencils (fulcrum), heavy books, string/rope, broom handle, spring scale or luggage scale.
**Estimated cost:** $0–8

## CHEMISTRY — CORE (C1–C14)

### C1: Acids and Bases
**Question:** Can you make an invisible chemical detector from a cabbage?
**What the student does:** Boil red cabbage to extract the pigment (anthocyanin). Pour the purple juice into small cups. Add household substances: vinegar, lemon juice, baking soda dissolved in water, soap, milk, bleach. Each turns a different color — vivid reds for acids, greens and yellows for bases.
**The surprise:** A single substance from a cabbage can detect whether something is acidic or basic — and the color tells you how strong. The range of colors is dramatic.
**Key observation:** Indicators change color based on pH. Acids donate hydrogen ions; bases accept them. pH is a scale measuring hydrogen ion concentration.
**Materials:** Red cabbage, pots for boiling, small cups, household substances (vinegar, lemon juice, baking soda, soap, milk, ammonia cleaner).
**Estimated cost:** $5

---

### C2: Electrochemistry
**Question:** Can you generate electricity from fruit?
**What the student does:** Insert a zinc-coated nail and a copper coin (or copper wire) into a lemon. Connect a multimeter — it shows voltage. Then try different fruits: potato, apple, banana, orange. Record the voltage from each. Wire two lemon cells in series and power a small LED.
**The surprise:** Fruit produces measurable electricity. The zinc is actually dissolving — you can see pitting on its surface after the experiment. Chemical energy is becoming electrical energy.
**Key observation:** The voltage comes from the difference in reactivity between the two metals. The acidic juice serves as an electrolyte, allowing ions to flow. This is oxidation (zinc loses electrons) and reduction (copper gains them).
**Materials:** Lemons, potatoes, other fruits, zinc nails or strips, copper wire or coins, alligator clip wires, multimeter, LED.
**Estimated cost:** $10–12

---

### C3: Chemical Reactions
**Question:** Can a chemical reaction be cold?
**What the student does:** Mix baking soda and vinegar in a bag — it inflates and gets warm (exothermic). Then dissolve citric acid and baking soda in water — it gets cold (endothermic). Measure temperature change with a thermometer in both cases. Compare the two reactions.
**The surprise:** Most people assume reactions are always hot. The endothermic reaction gets noticeably cold — sometimes dropping 10°+ below room temperature. Energy is being absorbed, not released.
**Key observation:** Chemical reactions either release energy (exothermic, temperature rises) or absorb energy (endothermic, temperature drops). The energy comes from breaking and forming chemical bonds.
**Materials:** Baking soda, vinegar, citric acid, ziplock bags, thermometer, measuring cups.
**Estimated cost:** $5

### C4: Atomic Structure
**Question:** Can you identify a mystery element by burning it?
**What the student does:** Dip wooden splints (or cotton swabs on wire loops) into solutions of different metal salts: lithium chloride (red), copper chloride (green/blue), sodium chloride (yellow), potassium chloride (violet), strontium chloride (red). Hold them in a flame and observe the colors. Then get an "unknown" and identify it by its flame color.
**The surprise:** Each element produces a completely unique color. The colors are vivid and unmistakable. This is the same principle used to make fireworks.
**Key observation:** When electrons absorb energy from the flame, they jump to higher energy levels. When they fall back, they emit light at specific wavelengths unique to each element. This is emission spectra.
**Materials:** Metal salt solutions (available in chemistry sets or online), wooden splints or nichrome wire loops, alcohol lamp or candle, safety goggles.
**Estimated cost:** $12–15

---

### C5: The Periodic Table
**Question:** Which metals react the fastest — and can you predict which ones from the periodic table?
**What the student does:** Place small pieces of zinc, copper, iron, and magnesium in separate cups of dilute hydrochloric acid (or strong vinegar). Observe: magnesium fizzes violently, zinc fizzes moderately, iron fizzes slowly, copper doesn't react at all. Rank them by reactivity.
**The surprise:** The reactivity order matches their position on the periodic table's activity series. You can predict chemical behavior from the table's organization.
**Key observation:** Elements in the same group share chemical properties. Reactivity correlates with how easily an atom loses its valence electrons. The periodic table is a predictive tool, not just a reference chart.
**Materials:** Small pieces of zinc, copper, iron, magnesium, vinegar or dilute HCl, small cups, safety goggles.
**Estimated cost:** $10–12

### C6: Bonding
**Question:** Why does salt dissolve in water but not in oil — and why does the salt water conduct electricity?
**What the student does:** Dissolve salt in water, then try to dissolve it in oil — it won't. Dissolve sugar in water — it does. Then test conductivity: put two electrodes (wires connected to a battery and LED) into each solution. Salt water lights the LED. Sugar water doesn't. Pure water doesn't.
**The surprise:** Both salt and sugar dissolve in water, but only the salt solution conducts electricity. This reveals that salt breaks into charged ions when dissolved, while sugar stays as intact molecules.
**Key observation:** Ionic compounds (like NaCl) dissociate into ions when dissolved, creating a conducting solution. Covalent compounds (like sugar) dissolve but don't produce ions.
**Materials:** Salt, sugar, cooking oil, water, cups, battery, LED, wire electrodes.
**Estimated cost:** $5

### C7: States of Matter
**Question:** Can you make water boil without heating it?
**What the student does:** Fill a syringe halfway with warm (not boiling) water. Seal the tip. Pull the plunger back to reduce pressure — bubbles form and the water boils at room temperature. Then observe dry ice sublimating directly from solid to gas (if available). Plot temperature over time while heating ice through melting and boiling — note the flat plateaus during phase transitions.
**The surprise:** Water boils at room temperature when you reduce the pressure enough. Boiling point isn't fixed — it depends on pressure. This is why water boils at lower temperatures at high altitudes.
**Key observation:** Phase transitions depend on both temperature and pressure. During a phase change, temperature remains constant while energy goes into breaking intermolecular bonds (latent heat).
**Materials:** Large syringe (60mL), warm water, thermometer, hot plate or stove, ice, pot, dry ice (optional, ~$5 from grocery store).
**Estimated cost:** $5–8

---

### C8: Gas Laws
**Question:** Can you crush a sealed container without touching it?
**What the student does:** Pour a small amount of hot water into an empty aluminum can. Heat until steam escapes. Quickly invert the can into a bowl of ice water — it crushes instantly with a loud crack. Then do the controlled version: attach a syringe to a pressure sensor (or just use a sealed syringe) and measure how volume changes when you push or pull the plunger. Plot pressure vs. volume. Then put a balloon in the freezer and watch it shrink; warm it and it expands.
**The surprise:** The can crushes violently — atmospheric pressure is enormously strong, we just don't notice it because it's balanced. The steam condensing inside created a partial vacuum.
**Key observation:** Gas volume, pressure, and temperature are interdependent. Boyle's law: as pressure increases, volume decreases (at constant temperature). Charles's law: as temperature increases, volume increases (at constant pressure).
**Materials:** Empty aluminum cans, tongs, bowl of ice water, hot plate/stove, syringe, balloons, freezer.
**Estimated cost:** $3–5

### C9: Solutions and Concentration
**Question:** Can you make crystals appear out of nowhere?
**What the student does:** Prepare a supersaturated sodium acetate solution (dissolve sodium acetate in hot water until no more dissolves, then carefully cool it). Pour it onto a surface or drop in a seed crystal — instant, dramatic crystallization spreading across the surface. Then grow slower crystals: dissolve as much sugar or salt as possible in hot water, hang a string in the solution, and observe crystal growth over several days.
**The surprise:** The supersaturated solution looks like plain water — but it's holding more dissolved material than it should be able to. A single seed crystal triggers a chain reaction of crystallization that's visually stunning.
**Key observation:** Saturation is the maximum amount of solute a solvent can hold at a given temperature. Supersaturation is an unstable state where the solution holds more than the maximum — any disturbance triggers rapid precipitation.
**Materials:** Sodium acetate (hand warmer refills or online), sugar, string, jars, hot water.
**Estimated cost:** $8–10

### C10: Reaction Rates
**Question:** How much faster does a reaction go when you raise the temperature?
**What the student does:** Drop an effervescent tablet (Alka-Seltzer) into cold water, room temperature water, and hot water. Time how long each takes to fully dissolve. Then test concentration: crush a tablet into powder and dissolve it vs. a whole tablet. Then test surface area: break a tablet in half, quarters, and leave one whole.
**The surprise:** Hot water dissolves the tablet dramatically faster — often 3–4× faster. Crushing it into powder also speeds it up enormously. Both effects are striking and immediate.
**Key observation:** Reaction rate increases with temperature (molecules move faster, more energetic collisions), increased concentration, and increased surface area (more contact between reactants). This is collision theory in action.
**Materials:** Effervescent tablets (Alka-Seltzer or generic), cups, water at different temperatures, thermometer, stopwatch.
**Estimated cost:** $5

### C11: Conservation of Mass
**Question:** Does anything actually disappear in a chemical reaction?
**What the student does:** Place baking soda and vinegar in a sealed ziplock bag on a scale. Mix them inside the bag (it inflates with CO₂). Read the scale — the mass is the same before and after. Then do an open reaction: weigh baking soda + vinegar in an open container. The mass appears to decrease — because the CO₂ gas escaped. Compare the two results.
**The surprise:** In the sealed bag, mass is perfectly conserved even though a gas was produced and the bag inflated. In the open container, mass "disappears" — but only because a product escaped into the air. Nothing was destroyed.
**Key observation:** Mass is conserved in all chemical reactions. Atoms are rearranged, not created or destroyed. The apparent loss of mass in open reactions is due to gaseous products escaping.
**Materials:** Baking soda, vinegar, ziplock bags, kitchen scale (precise to 0.1g or better), open containers.
**Estimated cost:** $5

### C12: Chemical Equilibrium
**Question:** Can you force a chemical reaction to reverse direction?
**What the student does:** Dissolve cobalt chloride in water — it's pink. Heat it — it turns blue. Cool it — it turns pink again. The reaction shifts back and forth depending on temperature. Then add concentrated HCl — it turns blue. Add water — it turns pink. The same reaction, pushed in different directions by different stresses.
**The surprise:** The reaction doesn't "finish" — it sits in a balance between two states, and you can push it either way. Heat, concentration, and pressure all shift the equilibrium. It feels like controlling a living system.
**Key observation:** Reversible reactions reach a dynamic equilibrium. Le Chatelier's principle: when a system at equilibrium is stressed, it shifts to partially counteract the stress.
**Materials:** Cobalt chloride (available online), water, hydrochloric acid (dilute), heat source, test tubes or clear cups, safety goggles and gloves.
**Estimated cost:** $12–15

### C13: Nuclear Chemistry
**Question:** Can you see radiation?
**What the student does:** Build a cloud chamber: place a shallow dish on a block of dry ice, line it with felt soaked in isopropyl alcohol, seal with a clear lid. Shine a flashlight from the side. Within minutes, you see tiny vapor trails — actual paths of alpha and beta particles from background radiation or a small radioactive source (like an americium-241 button from a smoke detector). Then simulate half-life: start with 100 coins. Flip them all. Remove all heads. Record how many remain. Repeat. Plot the decay curve.
**The surprise:** You can literally see subatomic particles leaving tracks in the vapor. The cloud chamber makes the invisible visible. And the coin simulation produces a perfect exponential decay curve even though each individual coin flip is random.
**Key observation:** Radioactive decay is random at the individual level but predictable statistically. Half-life is the time for half the atoms in a sample to decay. This is exponential, not linear.
**Materials:** Shallow dish, dry ice, isopropyl alcohol (99%), felt/sponge, clear plastic container, flashlight, 100 coins. (Optional: old smoke detector for americium source.)
**Estimated cost:** $10–15

### C14: Thermochemistry
**Question:** How many calories are really in a cheese puff?
**What the student does:** Build a simple calorimeter: a soda can filled with a measured amount of water, suspended over a burning cheese puff (or peanut, or other food). Measure the water temperature before and after. Calculate the energy released using Q = mcΔT. Compare to the nutrition label.
**The surprise:** The calculated value is always much lower than the label says — because a lot of heat escapes into the air. This demonstrates both calorimetry and systematic error in one experiment. The amount of energy in food is also surprising — a single peanut can raise water temperature by 10–20°C.
**Key observation:** Chemical bonds in food store energy. When burned (rapid oxidation), that energy is released as heat. Calorimetry measures energy transfer by tracking temperature change in water.
**Materials:** Soda can, water, thermometer, cheese puffs or peanuts, long needle or pin (to hold food), lighter, kitchen scale.
**Estimated cost:** $5

## BIOLOGY — CORE (B1–B12)

### B1: Cells
**Question:** What does the inside of your cheek look like under a microscope?
**What the student does:** Gently scrape the inside of their cheek with a toothpick. Smear on a slide, add a drop of methylene blue stain, and cover with a coverslip. Under the microscope: individual cells with visible nuclei. Then prepare an onion skin slide the same way — compare the two. Plant cells have rigid walls and a rectangular shape; cheek cells are round and blobby.
**The surprise:** Your body is made of millions of individual, visible units. And plant cells look fundamentally different from animal cells — rectangular with thick walls vs. round and soft.
**Key observation:** All living things are made of cells. Animal cells have a flexible membrane; plant cells have a rigid cell wall. Both have a nucleus containing genetic material.
**Materials:** Microscope (or phone microscope attachment ~$15), slides, coverslips, toothpicks, methylene blue stain, onion.
**Estimated cost:** $15–20 (if microscope attachment needed)

---

### B2: Microorganisms
**Question:** What's living on your kitchen counter right now?
**What the student does:** Prepare agar plates (or buy pre-poured ones). Swab different surfaces: phone screen, doorknob, kitchen counter, inside of mouth, toilet handle, a hand before and after washing. Seal the plates, label them, incubate at room temperature for 48–72 hours. Observe colony growth.
**The surprise:** The phone screen and doorknob plates are often more colonized than the toilet handle. The "after hand-washing" plate has dramatically fewer colonies than "before." The sheer number of bacteria everywhere is shocking.
**Key observation:** Microorganisms are ubiquitous. Different environments support different colony counts and types. Sterile technique and hand hygiene have measurable effects on microbial populations.
**Materials:** Agar plates (pre-poured or agar powder + petri dishes), cotton swabs, tape, marker, warm location for incubation.
**Estimated cost:** $12–15

---

### B3: Photosynthesis
**Question:** Can you watch a plant produce oxygen in real time?
**What the student does:** Place an aquatic plant (Elodea or similar) in a beaker of water under a bright light. Small bubbles begin rising from the leaves — that's oxygen. Count bubbles per minute under different conditions: bright light, dim light, darkness, with added CO₂ (a pinch of baking soda in the water).
**The surprise:** More light = more bubbles. Adding CO₂ = more bubbles. In darkness, the bubbles stop entirely. You're watching the inputs and outputs of photosynthesis in real time.
**Key observation:** Photosynthesis uses light energy to convert carbon dioxide and water into glucose and oxygen. Light intensity and CO₂ concentration are both rate-limiting factors.
**Materials:** Elodea or other aquatic plant (pet store), beaker or clear jar, desk lamp, baking soda, timer.
**Estimated cost:** $5–8

### B4: Respiration
**Question:** Is yeast alive — and can you prove it?
**What the student does:** Mix warm water, sugar, and active dry yeast in a bottle. Stretch a balloon over the top. The balloon inflates as the yeast produces CO₂. Then set up multiple bottles: yeast + sugar (inflates), yeast + no sugar (barely inflates), yeast + sugar + cold water (inflates slowly), dead yeast + sugar (nothing). Compare.
**The surprise:** The balloon inflates noticeably within 10–15 minutes. Yeast is alive, eating sugar, and exhaling CO₂ — it's doing cellular respiration. Without food (sugar), it barely produces anything. When killed by boiling first, nothing happens.
**Key observation:** Cellular respiration breaks down glucose to release energy, producing CO₂ as a byproduct. This process requires living organisms, a food source, and is affected by temperature.
**Materials:** Active dry yeast, sugar, bottles, balloons, warm/cold water, thermometer.
**Estimated cost:** $5

### B5: Cell Division
**Question:** Can you catch cells in the act of dividing?
**What the student does:** Take a growing onion root tip (place an onion on a cup of water for a few days until roots emerge). Cut 2mm of root tip, stain with toluidine blue or iodine, squash under a coverslip, and examine under a microscope. Different cells are in different stages of mitosis — some show clearly separated chromosomes, others show chromosomes lined up at the center.
**The surprise:** Not all cells are in the same state. You can see individual chromosomes in some cells. Cell division is happening right now in the root, and you froze different cells at different stages.
**Key observation:** Mitosis is the process of cell division where one cell becomes two identical copies. The stages (prophase, metaphase, anaphase, telophase) are visible under a microscope.
**Materials:** Onion, cup, microscope, slides, coverslips, iodine or toluidine blue stain, razor blade (with adult supervision).
**Estimated cost:** $3–5

### B6: Genetics Basics
**Question:** Can you pull DNA out of a strawberry with your bare hands?
**What the student does:** Mash strawberries in a bag. Add a mixture of dish soap (to break cell membranes), salt (to clump DNA), and water. Filter through cheesecloth. Add cold rubbing alcohol slowly — white, stringy DNA precipitates out. The student can spool it up on a wooden stick.
**The surprise:** DNA is a physical, visible substance. You can touch it. It looks like white, snotty threads. The concept that every living thing contains this molecule becomes viscerally real.
**Key observation:** DNA is a physical molecule contained in every cell. It can be extracted and observed. Dish soap breaks the cell membrane and nuclear envelope; alcohol causes DNA to precipitate out of solution.
**Materials:** Strawberries, ziplock bag, dish soap, salt, rubbing alcohol (cold), cheesecloth, small cup, wooden stick.
**Estimated cost:** $5

### B7: Homeostasis
**Question:** How does your body know how to keep itself the same?
**What the student does:** Measure their resting heart rate, then do 2 minutes of jumping jacks. Measure heart rate immediately after, then every 30 seconds until it returns to baseline. Plot the recovery curve. Then test pupil dilation: shine a flashlight in one eye and watch the other eye's pupil constrict too (consensual reflex). Measure body temperature at rest, after exercise, and after holding ice.
**The surprise:** Heart rate returns to baseline in a predictable curve — the body actively regulates itself. Both pupils respond even when only one eye gets light. Temperature barely changes despite exercise — the body is a thermostat.
**Key observation:** Homeostasis is the body's maintenance of stable internal conditions through feedback loops. When a variable deviates, the body detects the change and activates a response to restore equilibrium.
**Materials:** Stopwatch, flashlight, thermometer, mirror, pen and paper for recording.
**Estimated cost:** $0

### B8: Immune System and Disease
**Question:** Does hand-washing actually do anything?

### B10: Biogeochemical Cycles

### B12: Nervous System

### E4: Earth's Atmosphere and Weather

### E5: Climate Science

### E6: Earth's History and Geologic Time

### E8: Stars and the Universe

### AC1: Organic Chemistry Basics

### AC2: Electrochemistry (Advanced)

### AC3: Acid-Base Equilibria
**Question:** Why doesn't your blood's pH change when you eat acidic food?
**What the student does:** Prepare a buffer solution (mix of acetic acid and sodium acetate). Also prepare plain water. Add drops of strong acid (vinegar) and strong base (baking soda solution) to each and measure pH changes. The buffer barely changes. The water swings wildly. Then perform a titration: slowly add base to a known acid with an indicator, recording the color change at the equivalence point. Plot the titration curve.
**The surprise:** The buffer resists pH change remarkably well. You can add 10 drops of acid and the pH barely moves. In plain water, the same 10 drops cause a huge shift. Your blood works the same way — buffered to stay at pH 7.4.
**Key observation:** Buffers are solutions that resist pH changes by neutralizing added acid or base through equilibrium shifts. Titration determines the concentration of an unknown acid or base by reacting it with a known solution until the equivalence point.
**Materials:** Vinegar, baking soda, sodium acetate (available online), pH indicator or pH strips, dropper, graduated cylinder or syringe, cups.

### AC4: Redox and Corrosion
**Question:** Why do ships have blocks of zinc bolted to their hulls?
**What the student does:** Set up corrosion cells: place iron nails in salt water with different metals attached (zinc, copper, aluminum). Leave for 48–72 hours. The nail attached to zinc stays clean (the zinc corrodes instead — sacrificial anode). The nail attached to copper corrodes faster than the control. Also test protective coatings: coat nails with oil, paint, or nail polish and compare corrosion rates.
**The surprise:** Attaching zinc to iron makes the iron last longer by sacrificing the zinc. This is counterintuitive — you'd think adding a corroding metal would make things worse, not better. But the zinc preferentially loses electrons, protecting the iron.
**Key observation:** Corrosion is an electrochemical process (oxidation). More reactive metals corrode preferentially, protecting less reactive metals in contact with them. This is galvanic corrosion and the principle behind sacrificial anodes.
**Materials:** Iron nails (not galvanized), zinc strips, copper wire, salt, water, small containers, oil, paint or nail polish, timer.

### AB3: Plant Biology and Tropisms

### H4: Blood Sugar and Glycemic Response
**Question:** Do all carbohydrates hit your bloodstream at the same speed?
**What the student does:** Over four separate mornings (fasting overnight), eat a different food each day: white bread, whole grain bread, an apple, and cheese. Measure blood glucose at baseline, then every 15 minutes for 90 minutes using a glucometer. Plot four glucose curves on the same axes.
**The surprise:** White bread spikes glucose fast and high — then it crashes. Whole grain rises more slowly. The apple is in between. Cheese barely moves the needle. The student can feel the difference in energy and focus — the crash after white bread is real, and now they have the data.
**Key observation:** Carbohydrates vary in how quickly they raise blood glucose, depending on fiber content, molecular complexity, and what else is eaten alongside. The glycemic index quantifies this. For an ADHD learner, blood sugar stability directly affects sustained attention and executive function.
**Safety note:** Finger-prick glucometers involve a lancet. Instruct on proper use, hand washing, and safe disposal. If the student has a known blood sugar disorder, this module must be supervised by a medical professional.
**Connection to existing modules:** H1, H2. B7 (Homeostasis — blood sugar regulation is a negative feedback loop). Forward to H9, H10, H12.
**Materials:** Glucometer with test strips, white bread, whole grain bread, apple, cheese, timer, lab notebook.
**Estimated cost:** $20–30 (glucometer reusable across modules)

### H5: Micronutrients and Deficiency
**Question:** Which juice actually has the most vitamin C — and is it the one you'd guess?
**What the student does:** Prepare a DCPIP solution — a blue dye decolorized by vitamin C. Using a dropper, add juice drop-by-drop to a fixed volume of DCPIP until the blue color disappears. Count the drops. Fewer drops = more vitamin C. Test: fresh-squeezed orange juice, store-bought orange juice, lemon juice, apple juice, cranberry juice, and a vitamin C supplement dissolved in water (positive control). Then test whether boiling the juice destroys the vitamin C.
**The surprise:** Fresh-squeezed typically requires far fewer drops than store-bought. Lemon juice often beats orange juice. Apple juice has almost no vitamin C. Boiling destroys much of the vitamin C. Processing and heat matter enormously.
**Key observation:** Vitamins are organic molecules required in small amounts for essential biological functions. Vitamin C is an antioxidant destroyed by heat and degradation. Bioavailability depends on the food source and preparation. Whole, minimally processed foods deliver more actual nutrition.
**Connection to existing modules:** C1 (Acids and Bases — titration technique). C10 (Reaction Rates — heat increases degradation). H1 (macronutrients).
**Materials:** DCPIP solution (~$8), droppers, test tubes, various juices, vitamin C tablet, hot plate.
**Estimated cost:** $10–15

### H6: Exercise and the Cardiovascular System
**Question:** How hard does your heart actually work when you exercise — and how fast does it recover?
**What the student does:** Measure resting heart rate (1 minute, seated). Perform four exercise intensities in sequence with 5-minute recovery between each: slow walking (2 min), brisk walking (2 min), jogging in place (2 min), and maximum-effort burpees (1 min). Measure heart rate immediately after each bout, then every 30 seconds until within 10% of resting. Plot all four recovery curves on the same axes. Calculate estimated cardiac output at each intensity.
**The surprise:** Recovery time increases dramatically with intensity. After sprinting, 3–5 minutes to return to baseline; after walking, under 30 seconds. The recovery curve is exponential. Estimated cardiac output during maximum exercise is 5–6× the resting value.
**Key observation:** Cardiac output increases during exercise through higher heart rate and larger stroke volume. Recovery rate is one of the strongest indicators of cardiovascular fitness. It is measurable and improves with consistent training.
**Connection to existing modules:** B7 (Homeostasis). B4 (Respiration). Forward to H7, H11, AH3.
**Materials:** Heart rate monitor or manual pulse counting, stopwatch, space to exercise.
**Estimated cost:** $0 (uses existing equipment)

### H7: Strength Training and Muscle Adaptation
**Question:** Can you measure yourself getting stronger — and what does the science say about how it happens?
**What the student does:** Establish baseline measurements: max push-ups in one set, max wall-sit time, grip strength (dynamometer), and vertical jump height. Record all four. Learn progressive overload, specificity, and recovery. Begin a 3×/week bodyweight routine: push-ups (3 sets to near-failure), squats (3 sets of 15–20), and planks (3 sets to failure). Log every session. After 3 weeks of consistent training, retest all four baselines.
**The surprise:** Three weeks is enough to see measurable improvement — often 20–40% more push-ups and 15–30% longer wall sit. Grip strength is slowest to change. Strength is not a fixed trait — it responds predictably to progressive overload.
**Key observation:** Resistance training produces adaptation through neural adaptation (more efficient motor unit recruitment) and hypertrophy (muscle fiber cross-sectional area increase). Neural adaptation dominates in the first 4–6 weeks, explaining rapid beginner gains. Progressive overload is the non-negotiable requirement for continued adaptation.
**Connection to existing modules:** H6 (cardiovascular). B4 (Respiration — ATP and energy systems). H3 (protein for muscle adaptation). Forward to AH3, H12.
**Materials:** Stopwatch, measuring tape, wall for jump height, dynamometer, lab notebook.
**Estimated cost:** $0 (dynamometer in budget)

### H8: Dopamine, Screens, and Sustained Attention
**Question:** Does watching 30 minutes of short-form video measurably impair your ability to focus — and can you see it in your own data?
**What the student does:** Over three separate days (same time, same meal, same sleep — controlled variables), perform one pre-test activity for 30 minutes then immediately take a test battery. Day A: watch Instagram Reels/TikTok/YouTube Shorts for 30 minutes. Day B: sit quietly with no phone for 30 minutes. Day C: read a physical book for 30 minutes. Test battery: (1) 500-word reading comprehension passage + 5 questions (timed, accuracy recorded), (2) digit span test, (3) copy 50 random letters (time and error count). Plot all three metrics across three conditions.
**The surprise:** The difference is usually dramatic. After short-form video, reading comprehension drops, digit span shortens, and the copying task takes longer with more errors. The only variable that changed was the preceding 30 minutes. Quiet rest beats screens; book reading often produces the best subsequent test performance.
**Key observation:** Short-form video platforms deliver variable-ratio reinforcement — the same reward schedule used in slot machines. Over time, this produces dopamine receptor downregulation: ordinary activities feel understimulating by comparison. For an ADHD learner whose dopaminergic system is already atypical, this effect is amplified.
**Connection to existing modules:** B12 (Nervous System — dopamine pathways). H7 (discipline vs. instant gratification). H9 (screen time suppresses melatonin). H11 (overstimulation increases cortisol).
**Materials:** Phone or tablet, physical book, printed reading passages (3 copies, equal difficulty), digit span cards, printed random letter sequence, stopwatch, pen and paper.
**Estimated cost:** $0

### H9: Sleep and Circadian Biology
**Question:** Does how much you sleep actually change how well your brain works — and can you measure it?
**What the student does:** Over 5 consecutive days, log sleep duration each morning. At four time points each day, perform the ruler drop reaction time test (from B12) and a digit span test. Record all data. Plot reaction time and digit span against time of day, and separately against sleep duration.
**The surprise:** Two clear patterns emerge. Performance varies predictably across the day — most people have a mid-afternoon dip (the circadian trough). And nights with less sleep produce measurably worse reaction times and shorter digit spans. The student can see their own circadian rhythm and the dose-response relationship between sleep and cognition.
**Key observation:** Circadian rhythms are ~24-hour biological cycles regulated by the suprachiasmatic nucleus. Sleep deprivation measurably impairs reaction time, working memory, and executive function — the cognitive domains most affected in ADHD. Chronic sleep debt is not recoverable by "catching up" on weekends.
**Connection to existing modules:** B12 (Nervous System). B7 (Homeostasis). H11 (cortisol links stress and sleep).
**Materials:** Ruler, pen and paper for digit span test, phone for timing.
**Estimated cost:** $0

### H10: Skin, Sebum, and Acne
**Question:** Is there a measurable connection between what you eat and how oily your skin gets?
**What the student does:** For 7 days, press oil-blotting papers against four facial zones (forehead, nose, left cheek, right cheek) for 30 seconds each, at the same time each day. Rate each paper on a 0–4 oil scale. Log all food consumed each day, categorizing meals as high-glycemic, high-fat, or balanced. At the end, look for correlations between diet days and oil production the following day (~12–24 hour lag).
**The surprise:** High-glycemic days typically correlate with higher oil production the next day. High-fat days may or may not — dietary fat doesn't directly translate to facial oil the way most people assume. The T-zone produces significantly more oil than cheeks — sebaceous gland density is not uniform.
**Key observation:** Acne is an inflammatory condition driven by excess sebum production, hyperkeratinization, and bacterial colonization. High-glycemic diets increase insulin → androgen activity → sebum production. This is a measurable, evidence-based causal chain.
**Connection to existing modules:** H4 (blood sugar — insulin is the link). B2 (Microorganisms — skin microbiome). H1 (macronutrients). Forward to AH4.
**Materials:** Oil-blotting papers (~$5), printed oil rating scale, food diary template.
**Estimated cost:** $5

### H11: Stress Physiology
**Question:** Can you measure stress — and can you turn it off on command?
**What the student does:** Sit quietly for 3 minutes recording resting heart rate every 30 seconds (baseline). Perform a mental stress test: rapid serial subtraction (count backward from 500 by 7s, timed) for 3 minutes, recording heart rate. Then 3 minutes of guided breathing (4-sec inhale, 7-sec hold, 8-sec exhale), recording heart rate. Plot all three phases. Then test whether a 10-minute brisk walk beforehand reduces the stress response — repeat the protocol after the walk and compare.
**The surprise:** Heart rate rises measurably during mental arithmetic — even sitting still. The breathing exercise brings it down faster than just sitting. And the exercise pre-treatment blunts the stress response. You can consciously override the stress response, and exercise makes you more resilient.
**Key observation:** The sympathetic nervous system activates fight-or-flight; the parasympathetic counteracts it. Exercise and deliberate breathing are evidence-based tools for activating the parasympathetic system and improving stress resilience.
**Connection to existing modules:** B12 (Nervous System). H6 (Cardiovascular). H9 (cortisol links stress and sleep).
**Materials:** Heart rate monitor or manual pulse, stopwatch, quiet space, printed breathing guide. Optional: galvanic skin response sensor (~$20).
**Estimated cost:** $0–20

### AH1: Metabolism and Metabolic Rate
**Question:** How much energy does your body actually burn in a day — and where does it all go?
**What the student does:** Calculate basal metabolic rate using the Harris-Benedict equation with their own measurements (height, weight, age). Log all physical activity for one full day and calculate total daily energy expenditure using MET values. Compare to actual food intake from a 3-day food diary. Calculate the thermic effect of food (~10% of intake). Identify whether they are in approximate energy balance, surplus, or deficit.
**The surprise:** Most daily energy goes to BMR — just keeping the body alive. Exercise adds less than most people think. The thermic effect of food means digestion itself costs energy. The student sees exactly where their calories go.
**Key observation:** Basal metabolic rate accounts for ~60–70% of daily energy expenditure. MET values quantify the energy cost of different activities. Energy balance determines whether body mass increases, decreases, or stays stable.
**Connection to existing modules:** H1, H4, B4. Forward to AH4, AH5.
**Materials:** Calculator, measuring tape, scale, printed MET table, food diary.
**Estimated cost:** $0

### AH2: Gut Microbiome and Digestion
**Question:** What's living in your food — and does it matter for your gut?
**What the student does:** Culture bacteria from different fermented foods (yogurt, kimchi, sauerkraut) on agar plates. Compare colony morphology. Test pH changes during fermentation of milk with and without live cultures over 24 hours. Connect to probiotics and the gut microbiome's role in digestion and immune function.
**The surprise:** The colony diversity is striking — different foods harbor visibly different bacterial communities. Milk with live cultures drops in pH rapidly; the control stays roughly stable. Fermentation is bacteria eating and producing acid.
**Key observation:** The gut microbiome contains trillions of bacteria that aid digestion, produce vitamins, and modulate immune function. Probiotic foods contain live beneficial bacteria. Prebiotics are fibers that feed beneficial gut bacteria. Dysbiosis (microbial imbalance) is linked to inflammation and digestive disorders.
**Connection to existing modules:** H2, B2 (Microorganisms), C1 (Acids/Bases — pH).
**Materials:** Agar plates, fermented foods, pH meter, thermometer, milk, timer.
**Estimated cost:** $10–15

### AH3: Training Programming and Adaptation
**Question:** What happens to your body over 6 weeks of structured training — and can you see the supercompensation pattern in your own data?
**What the student does:** Follow a simple periodized bodyweight training program for 6 weeks (3 sessions/week, alternating push/pull/legs focus). Each week, measure: resting heart rate (Monday morning), push-up max, wall-sit time, and grip strength. Log all sessions. In week 4, reduce training volume to 50% (a deload week). Plot all metrics over the 6 weeks. Look for the supercompensation pattern: performance may dip during the deload, then jump above the previous baseline in weeks 5–6.
**The surprise:** The deload week often feels counterproductive — the student is doing less. But the data shows that adaptation accelerates after the deload. Rest is not laziness — it is when adaptation happens. This is the supercompensation principle made visible.
**Key observation:** Periodization — systematic variation of training volume and intensity — produces superior adaptation compared to linear progression. The supercompensation cycle (stress → fatigue → recovery → adaptation) is the fundamental mechanism of all training adaptation.
**Connection to existing modules:** H6, H7.
**Materials:** Dynamometer, stopwatch, measuring tape, training log.
**Estimated cost:** $0

### AH4: Nutrition, Inflammation, and Skin
**Question:** Can you measurably improve your skin by changing what you eat for two weeks?
**What the student does:** Two-week controlled comparison. Week 1: normal diet with daily skin oil measurements, food diary, and energy level rating (1–10). Week 2: deliberately reduce high-glycemic foods and increase omega-3 sources (fatty fish, walnuts, flaxseed); continue all daily measurements. Compare skin oil production, breakout frequency, and energy levels between weeks. Calculate average glycemic load per day for each week.
**The surprise:** Many students see a measurable reduction in skin oil production and an increase in self-reported energy during the controlled diet week. The mechanism is the insulin–IGF-1–androgen pathway: lower glycemic load → lower insulin → lower androgen-driven sebum production.
**Key observation:** Inflammatory cytokines and insulin-like growth factor (IGF-1) link diet to skin health through a measurable biochemical pathway. Omega-3 fatty acids have anti-inflammatory properties; omega-6 heavy diets promote inflammation. The ratio matters.
**Connection to existing modules:** H4, H10, AH1.
**Materials:** Oil-blotting papers, food diary template, glucometer, omega-3 rich foods.
**Estimated cost:** $15–25

### AH5: Building a Personal Protocol
**Question:** Based on everything you've measured about your own body — what's the optimal daily plan, and can you live it for a month?
**What the student does:** This is the capstone of the Health Science strand. Implement for 4 weeks, logging compliance and measuring key metrics (resting heart rate, strength benchmarks, reaction time, skin oil, energy rating). Write a comprehensive research report analyzing whether the protocol improved outcomes. Present as a portfolio defense piece.
**The surprise:** Four weeks of deliberate, evidence-based habits produce measurable, visible improvements across multiple metrics simultaneously. The student designed this protocol themselves using their own data, implemented it with discipline, and can see the results in their own measurements.
**Key observation:** An n-of-1 experiment is a controlled study on a single subject. Dose-response relationships describe how the magnitude of an input relates to the magnitude of an effect. Compliance — actually following the protocol — is the variable that determines whether the science works in practice. This module is the ultimate expression of the curriculum's philosophy: the student is doing science, not following instructions.
