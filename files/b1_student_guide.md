## 1. Phenomenon Hook

> You cut your knee and it heals. You grow six inches in a year. You eat food and turn it into muscle, bone, and brain tissue. How? Every part of your body — your skin, your blood, your brain, your muscles — is built from the same kind of building block: cells. Right now, roughly 37 trillion of them are working inside you. But what does a cell actually look like? Today you are going to look at your own cells under a microscope — cells scraped from the inside of your cheek — and compare them to plant cells from an onion. What you see will surprise you: the two look completely different, even though both are alive and both carry your complete DNA.

## 3. The Experiment

**Safety first:** Put on safety goggles and gloves. Methylene blue and iodine will stain your skin and clothing. If you get stain on your skin, wash immediately with soap and water — it will fade in a few hours but is difficult to remove from fabric. Handle glass slides and coverslips carefully — the edges are sharp. Dispose of the toothpick after use (do not share it — this is a biosafety practice). Do not eat the onion after the experiment.

Open your lab notebook to a fresh page. Write today's date and "Module B1" at the top.

---

### Step 1. Write your prediction.

Before you touch the microscope, answer this question in your notebook: **What do you think a human cell looks like? Draw a quick sketch of what you imagine.** Label any parts you think might be there. This is your prediction — there is no wrong answer.

---

### Step 2. Prepare the cheek cell slide.

Place a clean glass slide on the table. Add one small drop of water to the center of the slide using the dropper bottle.

---

### Step 3. Collect your cheek cells.

Take the flat end of the plastic toothpick. Open your mouth and gently scrape the inside of your cheek — press firmly and scrape slowly back and forth 5–6 times. You will not see anything on the toothpick, but cells are there.

---

### Step 4. Transfer the cells to the slide.

Stir the toothpick in the drop of water on the slide for about 10 seconds, rubbing it in small circles. The cells are now suspended in the water, invisible to the naked eye. Dispose of the toothpick.

---

### Step 5. Add the methylene blue stain.

Add one drop of methylene blue stain to the water on the slide. The stain will turn the water dark blue.

**Observe:** The stain is not decoration — it serves a critical purpose. Most cells are nearly transparent. Without stain, you would see almost nothing under the microscope. Methylene blue binds to structures inside the cell, making them visible by increasing contrast.

---

### Step 6. Place the coverslip.

Hold a glass coverslip at a 45-degree angle, touching one edge to the slide right next to the drop of stained water. Slowly lower the coverslip down onto the liquid. This technique pushes air bubbles out instead of trapping them under the glass.

---

### Step 7. View at low power (100×).

Place the slide on the microscope stage. Start with the lowest magnification objective (usually 4× or 10×, giving 40× or 100× total magnification with the eyepiece). Use the coarse focus knob to find the stained layer. You should see blue-stained shapes scattered across the field of view.

**Record:** Draw what you see at low power. Note the approximate number of cells visible and their general distribution (clumped, scattered, overlapping).

---

### Step 8. Switch to high power (400×).

Center a group of well-stained cells in your field of view. Switch to the high-power objective (40×, giving 400× total magnification). Use only the fine focus knob to sharpen the image. Do not use the coarse focus knob at high power — you may crack the slide.

**Observe:** You are now looking at individual human ***cells*** — the basic structural and functional units of all living organisms. Each blue-stained shape is a single cell from the lining of your mouth.

**Record:** Draw 2–3 individual cells at 400×. Draw them large enough to show detail.

---

### Step 9. Identify the cell structures.

Look closely at one well-stained cell. You should see:

- A dark blue circle near the center of the cell. This is the ***nucleus*** — the membrane-bound compartment that contains the cell's DNA. Every one of your cheek cells contains a complete copy of your entire genome.
- A lighter blue region surrounding the nucleus that fills the rest of the cell. This is the ***cytoplasm*** — the gel-like fluid where most of the cell's chemical reactions take place.
- The outer boundary of the cell. This is the ***cell membrane*** — a thin, flexible barrier that controls what enters and leaves the cell. It is so thin you may only see it as the edge where the cytoplasm meets the background.

**Record:** Label your drawing with these three structures: nucleus, cytoplasm, and cell membrane. If you can see any other structures, draw and label them too.

> *You just identified structures at a scale invisible to the naked eye. The transition from "I see a blue blob" to "I see a nucleus, cytoplasm, and membrane" is the transition from looking to observing.*

---

### Step 10. Prepare the onion cell slide.

Set your cheek cell slide aside. Take a clean slide and add one drop of water to the center.

---

### Step 11. Peel a thin layer of onion epidermis.

Cut the onion in half (or use a pre-cut piece). Find a fleshy inner layer. Using tweezers, peel off the thinnest possible layer of the transparent skin (epidermis) from the inner surface of one layer. It should be paper-thin and nearly see-through. You need a piece about 1 cm × 1 cm.

---

### Step 12. Place the onion epidermis on the slide.

Lay the onion epidermis flat in the drop of water on the slide. Use the toothpick or tweezers to smooth out any folds or wrinkles. The tissue should lie completely flat.

---

### Step 13. Add the iodine stain.

Add one drop of iodine stain to the onion epidermis. Wait 30 seconds for the stain to penetrate.

---

### Step 14. Place the coverslip.

Place the coverslip using the same 45-degree lowering technique as before.

---

### Step 15. View the onion cells at low power (100×).

Place the slide on the microscope stage. Focus at low power. You should see a brick-like pattern of rectangular cells, stained yellow-brown by the iodine.

**Record:** Draw what you see at low power. Note the shape and arrangement of the cells.

---

### Step 16. View at high power (400×).

Switch to high power and fine-focus on a group of clear cells.

**Observe:** The onion cells look dramatically different from the cheek cells. They are rectangular, not round. They are arranged in rigid, organized rows, not scattered loosely. And each cell has a clearly visible thick outer boundary that your cheek cells did not have.

That thick outer boundary is the ***cell wall*** — a rigid structure made of cellulose that surrounds the cell membrane in plant cells. Animal cells do not have a cell wall. The cell wall gives plant cells their rectangular shape and gives plant tissue its structural rigidity — it is the reason a celery stalk is stiff and your cheek is flexible.

**Record:** Draw 3–4 onion cells at 400×. Label the cell wall, cell membrane (inside the cell wall — very difficult to see as a separate structure), nucleus, and cytoplasm.

---

### Step 17. Look for organelles.

In both your cheek cell and onion cell drawings, the nucleus is the most visible ***organelle*** — a specialized structure within the cell that performs a specific function, just as an organ performs a specific function in the body. The nucleus is the organelle that stores and protects DNA.

One organelle you cannot see with a standard light microscope but that is present in every cheek cell and onion cell is the ***mitochondrion*** (plural: mitochondria). Mitochondria are the organelles where cellular respiration occurs — the process that converts the chemical energy from food (the same energy you measured in Module H1) into ATP, the molecule cells use as fuel. Every living cell that uses oxygen has mitochondria. Your cheek cells have hundreds of them; each is too small to resolve at 400× without special staining techniques.

**Record:** In your notebook, write a sentence explaining why you cannot see mitochondria in your slides, even though they are present in both cell types.

> *You just encountered the limit of your instrument. Recognizing what you cannot observe — and why — is as important as recognizing what you can.*

---

### Step 18. Build a comparison table.

In your notebook, create a table with three columns: **Feature**, **Cheek Cell (Animal)**, **Onion Cell (Plant)**. Include rows for: shape, cell membrane, cell wall, nucleus, cytoplasm, arrangement (scattered vs. organized), and approximate size (relative to field of view).

**Observe:** The two cell types share fundamental structures — both have a cell membrane, cytoplasm, a nucleus, and organelles including mitochondria. But the plant cell has a rigid cell wall that the animal cell lacks. This single structural difference explains an enormous amount about how plants and animals differ: why trees stand upright without bones, why you can bend your arm but can't bend a carrot, and why plant tissue crunches when you bite it.

**Record:** Complete the table with your observations.

## 5. Framework Connection

> You just observed the fundamental unit of life — the cell. Every living organism on Earth, from a bacterium to a blue whale, is made of cells. Robert Hooke first described cells in 1665 when he looked at cork under a microscope and saw empty chambers that reminded him of the small rooms (cellae) in a monastery. What you saw today was far more than empty chambers — you saw the nucleus containing DNA, the cytoplasm where chemistry happens, and the membrane that keeps it all contained.
>
> The difference between the cheek cell and the onion cell — the cell wall — is one of the most consequential structural differences in biology. Cell walls made of cellulose allow plants to build rigid structures (tree trunks, stems, leaves) without internal skeletons. Animals, lacking cell walls, evolved bones, cartilage, and muscle to achieve structural support. Two different solutions to the same engineering problem, visible under a $200 microscope.
>
> In Module B2, you will discover that not all cells have a nucleus. You will culture bacteria — single-celled organisms whose DNA floats freely in the cytoplasm with no nuclear membrane. In Module B5, you will watch cells in the act of dividing, seeing the chromosomes (DNA bundled inside the nucleus) separate in real time. And in Module B6, you will extract DNA itself — pulling the molecule out of cells and holding it in your hand.
