## Setup Checklist (Before the Student Arrives)

- **Assemble the Eisco food calorimeter.** Place the cylindrical metal stand on a heat-resistant surface (ceramic tile or metal tray). Set the metal suspension plate across the top. Place the Erlenmeyer flask on the plate. Confirm the cork-and-nail food holder fits inside the stand and sits below the flask. The whole assembly should be stable.
- **Pre-measure four portions of 100 mL water** in cups or beakers and set them on the workspace. This eliminates a repetitive step that slows momentum between trials.
- **Lay out all four food samples on a plate** with the nutrition labels (or printed nutrition info) beside each. Having the labels visible from the start saves time during Steps 17–19.
- **Test-burn one food sample yourself.** Mount an almond or cheese puff on the nail, light it, place it in the stand beneath the flask with 100 mL of water. Confirm the thermometer shows a clear temperature rise (expect 5–15°C for a cheese puff). If you get no temperature change, the food isn't burning long enough or the flask is too far above the flame.
- **Wrap the cork in aluminum foil.** Reviewer tip: the cork can char from repeated burns. A layer of foil prevents this and extends the kit's life.
- **Pre-zero the precision scale.** Set it on a flat surface and confirm it reads 0.0 g with nothing on it.
- **Insert the thermometer through the rubber stopper.** Test that it fits snugly and reaches the water when the stopper is in the flask.
- **Place the cup of water for fire safety within arm's reach of the student.** Not across the room.
- **Have the lab notebook worksheet and pen at the workspace.**
- **Ventilation:** Work near an open window or under a range hood if available. Burning food produces smoke and odor.

---

## Safety Notes

> **This is a moderate-risk experiment.** The open flame is small and brief, but requires active awareness. The Eisco food calorimeter kit is significantly safer than a DIY soda-can setup because the flask and stand are stable, the food holder is contained inside the metal cylinder, and the student's hands stay clear of the flame.
>
> **Fire safety:**
>
> - **Goggles are required.** Hot fat can splatter, especially from the cheese puff and almond.
> - **Hair must be tied back.** Non-negotiable with open flame.
> - **Keep the fire extinguishing cup of water within arm's reach at all times.**
> - **The food is lit outside the stand, then placed inside.** The student removes the cork-and-nail, lights the food with the lighter, then places the burning food back inside the metal stand. This keeps the flame contained.
> - **If a food sample falls off the nail while burning,** it will land on the heat-resistant surface inside the metal stand. Cover the top of the stand with the suspension plate to smother it, or douse with water.
> - **The Erlenmeyer flask contains hot water after each trial.** Remind the student to pour it out carefully (away from themselves) and let the flask cool for 30 seconds before refilling.
> - **Do not eat any food samples after the experiment.** Combustion residue is not food.
> - **Wash hands after handling burnt food and ashes.**

## Expected Results Reference

The student module does not include expected values — this is deliberate, so observations feel like genuine discoveries. Use this section to confirm results are in the normal range and to diagnose problems.

**Temperature rises (100 mL water, typical food sample sizes):**

| Food | Typical Mass | Expected ΔT | Expected Energy (kcal) | Expected kcal/g | Label kcal/g (approx.) |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Cheese puff | 1.5–2.5 g | 8–18°C | 0.8–1.8 | 0.4–0.8 | ~5.4 |
| Cracker | 3–4 g | 6–12°C | 0.6–1.2 | 0.15–0.4 | ~4.3 |
| Almond | 1.0–1.5 g | 8–20°C | 0.8–2.0 | 0.6–1.5 | ~5.8 |
| Marshmallow | 6–8 g | 5–12°C | 0.5–1.2 | 0.07–0.2 | ~3.2 |

**Key patterns the student should observe:**

- **Almond has the highest energy per gram.** This is the central finding — lipid is far more energy-dense than carbohydrate.
- **Marshmallow has among the lowest energy per gram.** Despite being large, it is mostly sugar and water.
- **All measured values are significantly below label values.** Expect 10–30% of the label value. This is the systematic error lesson — heat escapes, food doesn't fully combust, heat radiates sideways.
- **Cheese puff often surprises students** by burning vigorously with a bright flame. The fat content makes it highly flammable.

**Troubleshooting common results:**

- **ΔT = 0 or near zero:** Food didn't actually burn (just charred), flask is too far above the flame, or the food sample was too small. Fix: confirm the flask sits on the suspension plate directly above the food holder, use a larger sample, ensure the food catches and sustains a flame.
- **Very high ΔT (>25°C):** Usually the almond. This is normal — almonds burn hot and long due to high fat content. If it seems too high, check the starting temperature was recorded correctly.
- **Marshmallow melts instead of burning:** Common. The marshmallow may drip off the pin. Fix: skewer it more securely, or let it melt onto the heat-resistant surface and ignite it there. Some students may need to hold the lighter to it for longer.

## Timing Guide

This breakdown assumes a 16-year-old with ADHD. The experiment involves repetition (four burns), which can cause focus to fade — the key is keeping momentum between trials.

| Time | Activity | Your Role |
|---|---|---|
| **0–2 min** | Read phenomenon hook, write prediction | Say nothing. Let them read and write. |
| **2–5 min** | Assemble calorimeter, measure water (Steps 2–3) | Only intervene if the flask or stand looks unstable. |
| **5–7 min** | Record starting temp, weigh cheese puff (Steps 4–5) | If thermometer reading isn't stabilizing, check it's submerged. |
| **7–11 min** | Mount, light, burn cheese puff, record temps (Steps 6–9) | Supervise flame. Let them manage the cork-and-nail — don't do it for them. |
| **11–13 min** | Weigh remains (Step 10) | If they forget, prompt: "Let's see how much actually burned." |
| **13–20 min** | Cracker trial (Step 11) | This is where repetition can cause drift. Say: "Let's see if the cracker can beat the cheese puff." |
| **20–27 min** | Almond trial (Step 12) | The almond burns hot and long — this is the most dramatic trial. Let them watch. |
| **27–33 min** | Marshmallow trial (Step 13) | Marshmallow is anticlimactic after the almond. Good — the contrast is the point. |
| **33–38 min** | Calculate energy, build data table (Steps 14–16) | Help with the math only if asked. The formula is simple but the student may need a nudge on units. |
| **38–43 min** | Compare to labels, identify macronutrients (Steps 17–20) | Ask: "Which macronutrient does the almond have the most of? How does that connect to what you measured?" |
| **43–60 min** | Research report via digital tool | Available for terminology questions. Do not dictate. |

**If the student finishes the four burns quickly (under 25 min):** This is fine — some students are efficient. Move straight to the calculations. Do not add extra trials unless the student asks.

**If the student is losing focus during trials 3–4:** Frame it as competition: "The almond is next — I think it's going to crush the cracker. What do you think?"

## How to Facilitate Without Lecturing

### The Golden Rules (same as every module)

> - **Never explain before they observe.** If the student asks "What's a macronutrient?" before Step 19, say: "Let's finish the burns first. The labels will tell us."
> - **Ask, don't tell.** Instead of "Fat has more energy than sugar," ask: "Look at your data — which food had the highest energy per gram? Now look at the label — what macronutrient does it have the most of?"
> - **Let them struggle for 30 seconds before helping.** If the calculation is wrong, wait. They may catch the unit error themselves.
> - **Celebrate the data, not the "right answer."** If their almond measured lower than their cheese puff, that's still data. Ask: "Why do you think that happened? Did the almond burn completely?"
> - **Never touch their notebook.**

### Module-Specific Facilitation Notes

**On the systematic error conversation (Step 18):** This is the hardest concept in the module. The student will see that their numbers are way below the label values and may think they did something wrong. Reframe: "Your numbers are lower because heat escapes — but is every measurement low by about the same amount? That means the error is consistent. Your measurements can still tell you which food has more energy even if the absolute numbers are off. That's what systematic error means."

**On nutrition and diet:** The framing is always about fueling performance, not about restriction. If the student says anything that suggests concern about eating too many calories, redirect: "Calories are energy. Your body needs energy to think, train, grow, and recover. The question isn't 'how little can I eat' — it's 'am I giving my body the right fuel?' That's what the next few Health Science modules are about."

**On the almond surprise:** Many students will predict the marshmallow has the most energy (it's bigger, it's sugar, it's the "junk food"). When the almond wins, this is the key teaching moment. Don't explain it — ask: "The almond is tiny and the marshmallow is huge. But the almond has more energy per gram. What's different about what they're made of?"

## Common Mistakes and Troubleshooting

| Problem | Likely Cause | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| **Food won't ignite** | Lighter isn't hot enough; food is damp; not holding flame long enough | Hold lighter to food for 10–15 seconds. Try a different spot. Cheese puffs and almonds ignite easiest — start with those if struggling. |
| **Food burns but ΔT is tiny** | Flask is too high above flame; wind blowing heat away; food sample too small | Confirm the flask sits on the suspension plate directly above the food. If drafty, build an aluminum foil wind shield around the stand. Use a larger sample. |
| **Marshmallow melts off the nail** | Marshmallow softens rapidly when heated | Skewer it more firmly onto the nail. Or let it melt onto the cork (wrapped in foil) and ignite it there — the sugar will caramelize and burn. |
| **Temperature keeps rising after food goes out** | Residual heat in the flask and water | This is normal — take the "final" reading about 15 seconds after the flame goes out, when the temperature peaks. |
| **Student's calculated values are very low** | Likely correct — the calorimeter captures only 10–30% of the true energy. This is the systematic error lesson. | Confirm this is expected. Ask: "Where did the rest of the heat go?" |
| **Student mixes up calorie and kilocalorie** | Common — "calorie" on labels actually means kilocalorie | Use the label as the anchor: "The label says 160 Calories — that's 160 kilocalories, or 160,000 lowercase calories. Confusing? Yes. That's why we're being precise." |
| **Student rushes through calculations** | Wants to get to the report or is losing focus on math | Help them set up the first calculation, then let them do the rest. The table structure in Step 16 helps organize the work. |

## What to Watch For

### Signs of Understanding

- The student correctly explains why the almond has more energy per gram than the marshmallow, using the word "lipid" or "fat" and referencing the 9 vs. 4 kcal/g difference.
- The student identifies systematic error without prompting — "The numbers are all low because heat escapes."
- The student spontaneously connects to real life: "So if I ate almonds instead of marshmallows, I'd get more energy from less food?"
- The research report analysis cites specific temperature changes and calculated energy values.
- The student's next investigation question involves a variable they want to test (different foods, different amounts, a better calorimeter).

### Signs of Difficulty

- The student cannot explain why the almond outperformed the marshmallow — they see the data but can't connect it to macronutrient composition.
- The student thinks their low values mean they made a mistake, even after the systematic error explanation.
- The notebook has few entries — the writing barrier is too high during the repetitive burn trials.
- The student confuses calorie and kilocalorie in the calculations.

### If Difficulty Persists

Go back to the labels. Place the almond label and the marshmallow label side by side. Point to the fat content. Ask: "How many grams of fat in the almond? How many in the marshmallow? Now — fat has 9 kilocalories per gram and sugar has 4. Which food has more stored energy per gram?" Let the math do the work. Do not re-explain — let them recalculate.

## Research Report Coaching Reference

**Do not hand these to the student or read them aloud as answers.** Use them to ask guiding questions.

### Section 1: Research Question — What a Strong One Looks Like

> *"Does the macronutrient composition of a food affect its energy content per gram, as measured by calorimetry?"*

**What makes this strong:** It names the independent variable (macronutrient composition / type of food), the dependent variable (energy content per gram), and specifies the method (calorimetry). If the student writes "Which food has more calories?" ask: "What exactly are you changing? What are you measuring? How?"

### Section 2: Hypothesis — What a Strong One Looks Like

> *"I hypothesize that the almond will have the highest energy per gram because it is rich in lipid, which contains 9 kilocalories per gram — more than double the energy density of carbohydrate at 4 kcal/g. The marshmallow, which is mostly carbohydrate (sugar), should have lower energy per gram despite being larger in size."*

**What makes this strong:** It connects prediction to mechanism (lipid vs. carbohydrate energy density). If the student writes "The almond will have more energy because it's healthy," ask: "What specifically is in the almond that stores more energy? What's the energy density of that macronutrient?"

### Section 3: Experimental Design — What a Strong One Looks Like

> *"I burned four food samples (cheese puff, cracker, almond, marshmallow) one at a time in an Eisco food calorimeter with 100 mL of water in the Erlenmeyer flask. The independent variable was the food sample. The dependent variable was the water temperature change, which I used to calculate energy per gram. I controlled the volume of water (100 mL each time), the position of the flask above the flame, and the measurement technique."*

**If the student is stuck:** Ask: "What was the one thing you changed each time? What stayed the same, and why?"

### Section 4: Results — What a Strong One Looks Like

> *"The cheese puff (1.9 g) raised the water temperature by 12.4°C, yielding 1.24 kcal total and 0.65 kcal/g. The cracker (3.2 g) raised it by 8.7°C: 0.87 kcal total, 0.27 kcal/g. The almond (1.2 g) raised it by 15.1°C: 1.51 kcal total, 1.26 kcal/g. The marshmallow (7.1 g) raised it by 6.3°C: 0.63 kcal total, 0.09 kcal/g. All measured values were 15–25% of the label values."*

**Key coaching point:** Results report facts. Analysis interprets them. If the student explains why in this section, say: "Save the why for the next section. Here, just tell me exactly what happened — numbers, units, what you saw."

### Section 5: Analysis & Conclusion — What a Strong One Looks Like

> *"My hypothesis was supported: the almond had the highest energy per gram (1.26 kcal/g) because it is rich in lipid, the most energy-dense macronutrient at 9 kcal/g. The marshmallow had the lowest energy per gram (0.09 kcal/g) because it is almost entirely carbohydrate (sugar) at only 4 kcal/g. All measured values were below the nutrition label values due to systematic error — heat escaping the calorimeter into the surrounding air. Despite this, the ranking of foods by energy per gram in my data matched the ranking predicted by their macronutrient composition."*

**This is the most important section.** The requirement is at least 5 of 6 key terms used correctly and at least 2 specific data points. If the student writes "the almond had more energy because it's healthier," ask: "What macronutrient is the almond rich in? How much energy does that macronutrient contain per gram?"

### Section 6: Next Investigation — What a Strong One Looks Like

> *"I noticed that my measured values were only about 20% of the label values. My next investigation would test whether adding insulation to the calorimeter — wrapping the metal stand in aluminum foil and adding a foam sleeve around the flask — increases the percentage of energy captured. I would re-burn an almond with and without the insulation and compare the ΔT values. I predict the insulated version would capture at least 40% of the label value."*

**If the student writes "I'd try more foods":** Ask: "Which foods? What would you measure? What do you predict, and why?"

## Research Report Review Rubric

| Dimension | Strong | Needs Work |
|---|---|---|
| **Vocabulary Precision** | Uses 5–6 key terms correctly. "The almond's high lipid content explains its energy density of 1.26 kcal/g." | Uses colloquial substitutions: "the almond had more energy because of fat." Feedback: "What's the precise scientific term for fat as a macronutrient?" |
| **Evidence-Based Reasoning** | Cites specific data: "The almond (1.26 kcal/g) outperformed the marshmallow (0.09 kcal/g)." Connects data to macronutrient composition. | Makes claims without data: "The almond was the best." Feedback: "What was its measured energy per gram? How did that compare?" |
| **Investigative Thinking** | Proposes a specific follow-up with named variables and a prediction. | Vague: "I'd test more things." Feedback: "What variable would you change? What would you measure? What do you predict?" |

**Remember:** A Phase 1 report that uses 4 of 6 terms correctly is a success. The student just encountered these terms for the first time 40 minutes ago. Calibrate expectations accordingly.

## Content Knowledge Reference

You do not need to teach this material. This section exists so you can answer questions accurately if they arise.

**Why does burning food release energy?**

Food molecules contain chemical bonds. Breaking and reforming those bonds during combustion (or digestion) releases the energy stored in them. The specific process is oxidation — the same term from Module C2. The food reacts with oxygen, producing CO₂, H₂O, and heat. The body does the same thing through cellular respiration (B4), but captures the energy as ATP instead of releasing it all as heat.

**Why does fat have more energy per gram than carbohydrate or protein?**

Lipid molecules are more reduced (they have more C–H bonds relative to C–O bonds) than carbohydrate molecules. Since oxidation releases energy proportional to the number of electrons transferred, fat releases more energy per gram when fully oxidized. This is also why fats are hydrophobic — the lack of oxygen atoms makes them insoluble in water.

**The Q = mcΔT calculation**

Energy (in calories) = mass of water (g) × specific heat of water (1 cal/g·°C) × ΔT (°C). Since the specific heat of water is 1 cal/g·°C, the formula simplifies to: Energy (cal) = mass × ΔT. For 100 g of water: Energy (cal) = 100 × ΔT. Divide by 1,000 to get kilocalories.

**Why are measured values so much lower than label values?**

The Eisco food calorimeter captures only a fraction of the released energy. The primary losses are: (1) heat conducted into the air and metal stand, (2) incomplete combustion (food doesn't fully burn), (3) heat radiated sideways rather than into the flask, (4) heat lost through evaporation of water. A real bomb calorimeter seals the sample in pure oxygen inside an insulated container, capturing >95% of the energy. The student's calorimeter typically captures 10–30%.

**Calorie vs. kilocalorie confusion**

In everyday usage, "calorie" on a nutrition label actually means kilocalorie (kcal). The scientific calorie (lowercase) is the energy to raise 1 g of water by 1°C. The food Calorie (uppercase, on labels) is 1,000 of those — a kilocalorie. This is a genuine naming disaster in nutrition science, and it's worth naming that clearly to the student.

**Connection to AP content**

This module introduces concepts that align with AP Biology Unit 3 (Cellular Energetics) and AP Chemistry Unit 5 (Thermodynamics). The calorimetry technique, energy conservation, and bond energy concepts appear in AP Chemistry Learning Objectives ENE-2.A through ENE-2.D.

## Cleanup

- Pour out all heated water carefully. Let the flask cool fully before handling.
- Dispose of all burnt food remains and ash. Do not eat any food samples.
- If the cork is foil-wrapped, check whether the foil needs replacing for next use. Replace the nail if it is bent.
- Wipe down the heat-resistant surface, the metal stand interior, and the work area.
- Clean the thermometer and graduated cylinder.
- Return the precision scale to storage (turn it off).
- Ensure the lighter is stored safely (not left on the work surface).
- Store the Eisco calorimeter kit components together for reuse.
- Wash hands.
