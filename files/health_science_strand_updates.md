### H1: Macronutrients and Energy

**Question:** How much energy is actually hiding in your snack — and can you measure it?

**What the student does:** Using an Eisco Labs Food Calorimeter Kit (a purpose-built insulated calorimeter with a stirrer, thermometer port, and combustion platform), burn four different foods — a cheese puff (mostly fat and carbohydrate), a cracker (mostly carbohydrate), an almond (mostly fat and protein), and a marshmallow (mostly sugar). For each food: weigh the sample, measure 100 mL of water into the calorimeter's inner cup, record the starting water temperature, ignite the food on the combustion platform, stir the water throughout the burn, and record the final water temperature. Calculate energy per gram using Q = mcΔT. Then compare calculated values to the nutrition label values for each food.

**The surprise:** The almond contains far more energy per gram than the marshmallow — but the marshmallow is almost pure sugar. Fat is the most energy-dense macronutrient at 9 kcal/g, more than double carbohydrate and protein at 4 kcal/g each. The calculated values are always lower than the label because heat escapes — this is systematic error, revisited from earlier modules. The Eisco calorimeter captures more heat than a DIY soda-can setup, but still not all of it, making the gap between measured and label values a productive teaching moment.

**Key vocabulary introduced:** Macronutrient, carbohydrate, protein, lipid, calorie, kilocalorie

**Key observation:** Different macronutrients contain different amounts of stored chemical energy. A calorie is a unit of energy, not a property unique to food — it is the same energy unit used in chemistry and physics. Understanding the energy density of what you eat is the foundation of every nutritional decision you will ever make.

**Connection to existing modules:** Directly extends C14 (Thermochemistry/calorimetry). Uses the same Q = mcΔT calculation, but now applied to understanding nutrition rather than enthalpy. Also connects to B4 (Respiration) — cellular respiration is how the body extracts this stored energy.

**Materials:** Eisco Labs Food Calorimeter Kit (~$15–25), water, thermometer or temperature probe, cheese puffs, crackers, almonds, marshmallows, long pin or needle, lighter, precision scale, nutrition labels from each food.

**Estimated time:** 35–45 minutes (20 steps — requires setup, four separate burns with measurements, and calculation)

**Estimated cost:** $15–25 for the Eisco calorimeter (reusable across H1 and C14); $5–8 for consumable foods
