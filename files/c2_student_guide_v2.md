## 1. Phenomenon Hook

> Your phone is at 2%. Your controller is dead. The flashlight won't turn on. Every one of these problems comes down to the same thing: a battery that ran out of energy. But what actually IS a battery? What's happening inside that metal case that creates electricity from chemicals? Today you're going to build one — not buy one, build one — out of a lemon, two strips of metal, and some wires. By the end, you'll understand how every battery works, from the one in your TV remote to the one in a Tesla.

## 3. The Experiment

**Safety first:** Put on safety goggles. The voltages in this experiment are far too low to feel, but goggles are standard practice whenever you work with metal strips and acidic solutions. Protect your work surface with newspaper or a tray.

Open your lab notebook to a fresh page. Write today's date and "Module C2" at the top.

---

### Step 1. Roll the lemon.

Place the lemon on the table. Press your palm down on it and roll it back and forth firmly, five or six times. You are breaking the internal membranes inside the lemon to release juice. The lemon should feel noticeably softer when you are done.

---

### Step 2. Insert the zinc strip.

Pick up one zinc strip. Push it into the lemon about halfway — roughly 2.5 cm deep. Leave the other half sticking out so you can connect to it later.

---

### Step 3. Insert the copper strip.

Pick up one copper strip. Push it into the lemon about halfway, approximately 3 cm away from the zinc strip. The two strips must not touch each other inside the fruit.

---

### Step 4. Sketch your setup.

In your lab notebook, draw what you just built. Label the lemon, the zinc strip, and the copper strip. Show the approximate distance between the two strips.

---

### Step 5. Set the multimeter to DC voltage.

Look at the dial on the multimeter. Turn it to the setting labeled **DCV** or **V** with a straight line above it (not the wavy line — that is AC). If the multimeter is auto-ranging, just select the V⎓ setting. If it has manual ranges, choose the 2V or 20V range.

---

### Step 6. Measure the voltage.

Touch the **red probe** to the **copper strip**. Touch the **black probe** to the **zinc strip**. Read the number on the display.

**Observe:** The multimeter shows a positive number. This means electrical potential exists between the two metals — energy is available to push electrons through a circuit. That potential difference is called ***voltage***: the measure of how much energy is available to push electrons through a circuit, measured in volts (V).

**Record:** Write the exact voltage reading in your notebook, to two decimal places (for example: 0.92V).

> *You just made a quantitative observation — a measurement expressed as a number with units.*

---

### Step 7. Remove the zinc strip and examine it.

Carefully pull the zinc strip out of the lemon. Hold it up and look closely at the portion that was inside the fruit. Now compare it to the end that stayed outside.

**Observe:** The submerged portion looks different — it may be rougher, pitted, or discolored compared to the clean end that stayed outside. Here is what is happening at the atomic level: zinc atoms are each losing two electrons and entering the citric acid solution as zinc ions (Zn²⁺). This process — the loss of electrons — is called ***oxidation***. The zinc strip is the ***electrode*** where oxidation occurs, which makes it the anode.

**Record:** Describe in your notebook what the zinc surface looks like. Sketch the difference between the submerged end and the exposed end.

> *You just connected a visible observation to an invisible mechanism — the bridge between observation and theory.*

---

### Step 8. Look at the copper strip.

Look closely at the copper strip that is still inside the lemon. Focus on the part of the surface that is submerged in the fruit.

**Observe:** You may see tiny bubbles forming on the copper surface. If the zinc is losing electrons, where are those electrons going? They flow through the external circuit (the wires and multimeter) toward the copper strip. At the copper surface, hydrogen ions (H⁺) from the citric acid gain those electrons and become hydrogen gas (H₂). This process — the gain of electrons — is called ***reduction***. The copper strip is the electrode where reduction occurs, which makes it the cathode.

Together, oxidation at one electrode and reduction at the other form a complete ***redox reaction***.

**Record:** Note whether you see bubbles on the copper. Write down your observation either way — "no visible bubbles" is valid data.

---

### Step 9. Test what happens without the fruit.

Remove both strips from the lemon. Press the zinc strip and the copper strip directly together, metal to metal, with no fruit between them. Touch the multimeter probes to the strips and read the display.

**Observe:** The voltage should drop to zero or near zero. The citric acid solution inside the lemon was doing essential work: it contains ions (charged particles) that carry charge between the two electrodes inside the fruit, completing the internal circuit. A substance that produces ions when dissolved, allowing a solution to conduct electricity, is called an ***electrolyte***. Without the electrolyte, no current flows.

**Record:** Write the voltage reading with no fruit. Compare it to your lemon reading from Step 6.

> *You just performed a negative control — removing one variable to confirm it is necessary for the result.*

---

### Step 10. Set up the potato cell.

Take a fresh zinc strip and a fresh copper strip (or sand your used ones clean with sandpaper). Push them into the potato — same depth as before (about halfway), about 3 cm apart, not touching inside the potato.

---

### Step 11. Measure the potato voltage.

Touch the red probe to the copper strip and the black probe to the zinc strip. Read the multimeter.

**Record:** Write the voltage in your notebook.

---

### Step 12. Set up and measure the apple cell.

Repeat with the apple. Fresh or sanded strips, same depth, same spacing.

**Record:** Write the voltage in your notebook.

---

### Step 13. Set up and measure the orange cell.

Repeat with the orange. Fresh or sanded strips, same depth, same spacing.

**Record:** Write the voltage in your notebook.

---

### Step 14. Build a data table.

In your notebook, create a table with three columns: **Fruit**, **Voltage (V)**, and **Observations**. Fill in all four rows (lemon, potato, apple, orange) using the data you just collected.

**Observe:** The voltages are different. Each fruit has a different acid concentration and ion composition, which means a different strength of electrolyte.

> *You just ran a comparative experiment — changing one variable (the fruit) while keeping everything else constant (electrode material, depth, spacing). The variable you change is the independent variable. The variable you measure is the dependent variable.*

---

### Step 15. Set up two lemon cells.

You are going to wire two lemon cells together to increase the voltage. Set up two fresh lemon cells side by side, each with a zinc strip and a copper strip inserted. (You can reuse the original lemon if it still has good insertion holes, or use a second lemon if available.)

---

### Step 16. Wire the cells in series.

Use an alligator clip lead to connect the **copper strip of Cell 1** to the **zinc strip of Cell 2**. This connects the two cells in series.

---

### Step 17. Connect the LED.

Use an alligator clip lead to connect the **remaining zinc strip** (Cell 1, the unconnected one) to the **short leg** of the LED. Use another alligator clip lead to connect the **remaining copper strip** (Cell 2, the unconnected one) to the **long leg** of the LED.

**Observe:** The LED should glow. It may be dim — that is expected. Chemical energy in the fruit is converting to electrical energy, which is converting to light energy.

---

### Step 18. Measure the series voltage.

Disconnect the LED. Touch the multimeter probes across the full series circuit: red probe on the copper strip of Cell 2, black probe on the zinc strip of Cell 1. Read the display.

**Record:** Write the combined voltage. Compare it to the sum of your individual lemon cell readings. Reconnect the LED and observe the glow again.

**Record:** In your notebook, sketch the complete series circuit. Label both cells, both zinc strips, both copper strips, the alligator clip leads, and the LED. Show which leg of the LED connects to which strip.

---

### Step 19. Measure the current.

Turn the multimeter dial to **DC milliamps** (the setting labeled **mA** or **A** with a straight line). You may also need to move the red probe to a different jack on the multimeter — look for one labeled **mA** or **A**.

Disconnect one alligator clip lead from the LED circuit. Insert the multimeter in that gap so that current must flow *through* the meter to reach the LED. Read the display.

**Observe:** The current is very small — likely less than 2 milliamps. This is why the LED glows dimly. The fruit battery has high internal resistance: the electrolyte is weak and the electrode surface area is small. Voltage tells you the "push." Current tells you how many electrons are actually flowing per second.

**Record:** Write the current in milliamps. Describe the LED brightness (dim, moderate, barely visible).

> *You just measured two different properties of the same circuit: voltage (the driving force) and current (the actual flow). Distinguishing between these is a fundamental skill in physics and chemistry.*

## 4. Key Terminology

Five terms were introduced during this experiment. Each is anchored to a specific observation you made.

| Term | Definition | You Observed It When... |
|---|---|---|
| **Voltage** | The electrical potential difference between two points, measured in volts. It represents the energy available to push electrons through a circuit. | The multimeter displayed a reading between the zinc and copper strips in the lemon (Step 6). |
| **Oxidation** | The loss of electrons by an atom, ion, or molecule during a chemical reaction. | The zinc strip showed surface changes after sitting in the lemon — zinc atoms lost electrons and entered the solution as Zn²⁺ ions (Step 7). |
| **Reduction** | The gain of electrons by an atom, ion, or molecule during a chemical reaction. | Tiny bubbles may have appeared on the copper strip inside the lemon — hydrogen ions gained electrons and became hydrogen gas (Step 8). |
| **Electrolyte** | A substance that produces ions when dissolved, allowing the solution to conduct electricity. | When you removed the fruit and pressed the metal strips together, the voltage dropped. The citric acid solution was conducting ions between the electrodes (Step 9). |
| **Electrode** | A conductor through which electricity enters or leaves a solution or substance. The anode is where oxidation occurs; the cathode is where reduction occurs. | The zinc strip (anode) and the copper strip (cathode) were the two electrodes in each fruit cell (Steps 6–8). |

## 5. Framework Connection

> You just built a galvanic cell — the simplest form of an electrochemical cell, and the exact same operating principle behind every commercial battery. The AA battery in your TV remote? Two different metals and an electrolyte paste, producing voltage through the same redox reaction you just observed. The lithium-ion battery in your phone? A more advanced version — different electrode materials, a liquid electrolyte, but the same fundamental mechanism: oxidation at one electrode, reduction at the other, electrons flowing through the external circuit to do useful work.
>
> The voltage you measured depends on the standard electrode potentials of the two metals — tabulated values that quantify how strongly each metal tends to lose or gain electrons. Zinc and copper happen to produce about 1.1V in theory; you measured less because fruit is a weak electrolyte with high internal resistance.
>
> In Module AC2, you will reverse this process entirely: instead of a spontaneous redox reaction producing electricity, you will use electricity to force a non-spontaneous redox reaction — electroplating metal onto a surface. That reversal is the difference between a galvanic cell and an electrolytic cell, and it is the foundation of industrial electrochemistry.
