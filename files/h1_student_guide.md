## 1. Phenomenon Hook

> You grab a handful of almonds. Your friend grabs a bag of cheese puffs. Same size snack. But one of those snacks contains more than double the energy of the other — enough to power your body for an extra hour. Which one? And what does "energy in food" even mean? Today you're going to set food on fire, measure exactly how much energy comes out, and discover that not all calories are created equal. By the end, you'll understand what a calorie actually is, why some foods make you crash and others keep you going, and how to read a nutrition label like a scientist.

## 3. The Experiment

**Safety first:** Put on safety goggles. You will be working with open flames in this experiment. Keep hair tied back. Place the entire calorimeter on the heat-resistant surface. Keep the cup of water within arm's reach at all times — if any food sample flames unexpectedly, cover it with the metal suspension plate or douse it with water. Do not leave burning food unattended.

Open your lab notebook to a fresh page. Write today's date and "Module H1" at the top.

---

### Step 1. Write your prediction.

Before you touch any equipment, answer this question in your notebook: **Which food sample do you think contains the most energy per gram — the cheese puff, the cracker, the almond, or the marshmallow?** Write your prediction and explain your reasoning in one or two sentences.

---

### Step 2. Assemble the calorimeter.

Place the cylindrical metal stand on the heat-resistant surface. Set the metal suspension plate across the top of the stand. Place the Erlenmeyer flask on the suspension plate. The cork-and-nail food holder goes inside the stand, directly beneath the flask — this is where the burning food will sit.

---

### Step 3. Add water to the flask.

Use the graduated cylinder to measure exactly 100 mL of room-temperature water. Pour it into the Erlenmeyer flask. Place the rubber stopper (with the thermometer inserted through it) into the top of the flask.

---

### Step 4. Record the starting water temperature.

Place the thermometer into the water inside the flask (through the rubber stopper). Wait 30 seconds for the reading to stabilize. Read the temperature.

**Record:** Write the starting temperature in your notebook to one decimal place (for example: 22.3°C).

---

### Step 5. Weigh the cheese puff.

Place one cheese puff on the precision scale. Read the mass.

**Record:** Write the mass of the cheese puff in grams to one decimal place (for example: 1.8 g).

---

### Step 6. Mount the cheese puff on the food holder.

Push the nail on the cork food holder through the cheese puff so it is held securely above the cork. Place the cork-and-nail assembly inside the metal stand, directly beneath the flask.

---

### Step 7. Light the cheese puff.

Remove the cork-and-nail assembly from the stand. Hold the lighter flame to the bottom edge of the cheese puff until it catches fire on its own. This may take 5–10 seconds. Once it is burning independently, remove the lighter and quickly place the cork back inside the metal stand beneath the flask.

**Observe:** The cheese puff burns with a visible flame. Some foods burn brightly; others smolder. The energy stored in the food's chemical bonds is being released as heat and light.

---

### Step 8. Let it burn.

The burning food is now directly beneath the flask. Heat rises from the flame into the water above. Let the food burn until the flame goes out completely. Do not relight it.

---

### Step 9. Record the final water temperature.

As soon as the food has finished burning, gently swirl the flask (or stir through the stopper if your thermometer allows) and read the temperature.

**Record:** Write the final water temperature in your notebook. Write the temperature change (final minus starting temperature) on the same line.

> *You just performed calorimetry — measuring the energy content of a substance by capturing the heat it releases and measuring the temperature change in a known mass of water.*

---

### Step 10. Weigh the cheese puff remains.

Carefully remove the cork-and-nail assembly from the stand. Remove the burnt remains from the nail and place them on the scale. Some material may have fallen as ash — that is expected.

**Record:** Write the mass of the remains. Calculate and record the mass of food that actually burned (starting mass minus remaining mass).

---

### Step 11. Repeat with the cracker.

Pour out the heated water. Rinse the flask. Measure a fresh 100 mL of room-temperature water and pour it into the flask. Record the new starting temperature. Weigh the cracker, mount it on the nail, light it outside the stand, place it back inside, let it burn, record the final temperature. Weigh the remains.

**Record:** All data for the cracker — starting mass, starting water temperature, final water temperature, temperature change, remaining mass.

---

### Step 12. Repeat with the almond.

Same procedure. Fresh water, new starting temperature, weigh, burn, record.

**Record:** All data for the almond.

---

### Step 13. Repeat with the marshmallow.

Same procedure. Fresh water, new starting temperature, weigh, burn, record.

**Record:** All data for the marshmallow.

> *You just ran a comparative experiment — changing one variable (the food sample) while keeping everything else constant (water volume, calorimeter setup, measurement technique). The food sample is your independent variable. The temperature change is your dependent variable.*

---

### Step 14. Calculate the energy for each food.

Use this formula for each food sample:

**Energy (calories) = mass of water (grams) × temperature change (°C) × 1**

You used 100 mL of water, which has a mass of 100 g. So:

**Energy (calories) = 100 × ΔT**

This gives you energy in **calories** (lowercase c). To convert to **kilocalories** — which is what nutrition labels call a "Calorie" (uppercase C) — divide by 1,000. A ***calorie*** is the amount of energy needed to raise the temperature of 1 gram of water by 1°C. A ***kilocalorie*** equals 1,000 calories and is the unit used on every nutrition label you have ever read.

**Record:** For each food, write the calculated energy in calories and in kilocalories.

---

### Step 15. Calculate energy per gram.

For each food, divide the total energy (in kilocalories) by the mass of food that burned (in grams). This gives you kilocalories per gram — the **energy density** of each food.

**Record:** Write the energy per gram for all four foods.

---

### Step 16. Build a data table.

In your notebook, create a table with these columns: **Food**, **Mass Burned (g)**, **ΔT (°C)**, **Energy (kcal)**, **Energy per gram (kcal/g)**, **Label value (kcal/g)**. Fill in the first five columns from your data. Leave the last column empty for now.

---

### Step 17. Look up the nutrition labels.

For each food, find the nutrition label (from the packaging or the printed info). Calculate the kilocalories per gram from the label: divide the calories per serving by the serving size in grams.

**Record:** Fill in the "Label value" column in your data table.

---

### Step 18. Compare your measurements to the labels.

Look at your data table. Compare your measured energy per gram to the label value for each food.

**Observe:** Your measured values are lower than the label values — probably significantly lower. Here is why: your calorimeter is not perfect. Heat escapes into the air instead of going into the water. Some of the food did not burn completely. Some heat radiated sideways instead of going into the flask. This consistent undercount is called ***systematic error*** — a flaw in the experimental setup that skews all results in the same direction. Your numbers are still useful for comparing foods to each other, because the same error affects every measurement.

**Record:** For each food, calculate how much lower your measurement was than the label value (as a percentage). Write this in your notebook.

---

### Step 19. Identify the macronutrients.

Look at the nutrition labels again. Each label breaks the food into three main categories: fat, carbohydrates, and protein. These three are the ***macronutrients*** — the large-scale nutrient molecules that provide your body with energy.

- ***Carbohydrate*** — sugars and starches. Your body breaks them down into glucose for quick energy. Energy density: **4 kcal per gram**.
- ***Protein*** — chains of amino acids. Your body uses them to build and repair tissue, especially muscle. Energy density: **4 kcal per gram**.
- ***Lipid*** (fat) — the most energy-dense macronutrient. Your body uses it for long-term energy storage, hormone production, and cell membrane structure. Energy density: **9 kcal per gram**.

**Record:** For each food, write down which macronutrient dominates (the one with the highest gram count on the label). The cheese puff is mostly fat and carbohydrate. The cracker is mostly carbohydrate. The almond is mostly fat and protein. The marshmallow is almost pure sugar (carbohydrate).

---

### Step 20. Connect macronutrient composition to your results.

Look at your energy-per-gram rankings. The almond should have the highest energy per gram. The marshmallow should have among the lowest.

**Observe:** This makes sense now. The almond is rich in lipid, which contains 9 kcal/g — more than double the energy density of carbohydrate or protein at 4 kcal/g each. The marshmallow is almost pure sugar — a carbohydrate at 4 kcal/g. A small handful of almonds contains more chemical energy than a large marshmallow, because fat is a far more concentrated energy source than sugar.

**Record:** In your notebook, write one sentence explaining why the almond has more energy per gram than the marshmallow, using the terms macronutrient, lipid, and kilocalorie.

> *You just connected a quantitative observation (measured energy per gram) to a molecular explanation (macronutrient composition). This is the bridge between data and understanding.*

## 5. Framework Connection

> You just performed calorimetry — the same technique used by food scientists to determine the calorie counts printed on every nutrition label. The method you used is a simplified version of bomb calorimetry, where food is burned in a sealed, oxygen-rich chamber surrounded by water, and the temperature rise gives a precise measure of the food's total chemical energy.
>
> The energy you measured is stored in chemical bonds — the same bonds you studied in chemistry. When those bonds break during combustion (or during digestion), the energy is released. Your body does exactly what the flame did: it breaks down macronutrients through cellular respiration, releasing energy that gets captured as ATP rather than escaping as heat and light.
>
> Understanding energy density is the foundation of every nutritional decision. A food's calorie count is not arbitrary — it is determined by its macronutrient composition: lipid at 9 kcal/g, carbohydrate and protein at 4 kcal/g. In Module H3, you will use this knowledge to calculate your own daily protein needs and plan meals that actually hit the target. In Module H4, you will measure what happens to your blood sugar after eating different types of carbohydrates — and discover that not all carbohydrates affect your body the same way.
