## 1. Phenomenon Hook

> You are holding a bowling ball in one hand and a tennis ball in the other. You drop them both from the same height at exactly the same time. Which one hits the ground first? Almost everyone says the bowling ball — it's heavier, so it should fall faster. Galileo said they were wrong, almost 400 years ago. Today you are going to test it yourself, measure the results with a phone accelerometer, and discover what force, mass, and acceleration actually mean. What you find will change how you think about why things move — or don't.

## 2. Materials

**Equipment**

- **A clear drop zone:** a hard floor (tile, wood, concrete) at least 1.5 m below a stable dropping point

*Optional: slow-motion video recording on phone, additional objects of varying mass*

## 3. The Experiment

**Safety first:** Put on safety goggles. You will be dropping objects from above your head height. Make sure no one is standing in the drop zone. Work on a hard floor — carpet absorbs impact and makes timing unreliable. Do not drop anything heavy enough to damage the floor or your feet. Keep feet clear of the drop zone.

Open your lab notebook to a fresh page. Write today's date and "Module P1" at the top.

---

### Step 1. Write your prediction.

Before you touch any equipment, answer this question in your notebook: **If you drop a heavy object and a light object from the same height at the same time, which hits the ground first?** Write your prediction and explain your reasoning in one or two sentences.

---

### Step 2. Weigh all three objects.

Place each of the three objects (full water bottle, book, empty bottle) on the scale one at a time.

**Record:** Write the mass of each object in grams in your notebook. Convert each to kilograms by dividing by 1,000 (you will need kilograms later).

---

### Step 3. Mark your drop height.

Use the measuring tape to measure a height of exactly 1.5 meters from the floor. Mark this height on a wall or door frame with a small piece of tape. Every drop in this experiment will be from this same height.

---

### Step 4. Drop the heavy object and listen.

Stand at the marked height. Hold the full water bottle at exactly 1.5 m with the bottom of the bottle at the tape mark. Let go cleanly — do not push it down, do not toss it. Just open your fingers.

**Observe:** The bottle accelerates toward the ground and hits with a clear impact. What you just observed is ***acceleration*** — a change in velocity over time. The bottle started with zero velocity (at rest in your hand) and sped up continuously until it hit the floor. A ***force*** was pulling it downward the entire time: gravity.

**Record:** Write what you observed — describe the fall. Did it speed up gradually or seem to fall at constant speed?

> *You just made a qualitative observation — describing what happened in words, without numbers yet.*

---

### Step 5. Drop the light object from the same height.

Hold the empty water bottle at the same 1.5 m mark. Let go the same way.

**Observe:** It falls and hits the ground. Did it appear to take the same amount of time as the heavy bottle, or longer?

**Record:** Write your observation. Be honest — it may have looked like the same time, or the light bottle may have seemed slightly slower.

---

### Step 6. Drop both objects simultaneously.

This is the key test. Hold the full water bottle in one hand and the empty bottle in the other. Position both at the 1.5 m mark. Release both at exactly the same time.

**Observe:** Listen for the impacts. If you can, have someone else watch from the side, or record it on slow-motion video. The two objects should hit the ground at nearly the same time — despite having wildly different masses.

**Record:** Write exactly what you heard and saw. Did they hit at the same time? If not, which hit first, and by how much?

---

### Step 7. Test with two books.

Pick up the two hardcover books of different mass. Hold one in each hand at the 1.5 m mark. Drop both simultaneously.

**Observe:** Again — they should hit at nearly the same time.

**Record:** Write your observation.

> *You just replicated your finding with different objects. Replication — testing the same principle with different materials — strengthens the evidence for a conclusion.*

---

### Step 8. Now drop a book and a sheet of paper.

Hold the hardcover book in one hand and a single flat sheet of paper in the other. Drop both from 1.5 m simultaneously.

**Observe:** The book falls fast. The paper floats down slowly, drifting and fluttering. This looks like it contradicts what you just found — shouldn't they fall at the same rate?

**Record:** Write what happened. Estimate how much longer the paper took to land.

---

### Step 9. Crumple the paper into a tight ball.

Take the sheet of paper and crumple it into the tightest ball you can make. Now hold the crumpled paper ball in one hand and the book in the other. Drop both from 1.5 m simultaneously.

**Observe:** This time, they hit at nearly the same time. The paper still has the same mass as before — you didn't add or remove anything. What changed is its shape, which changed how much air resistance (drag) it experienced. The flat sheet pushed against a lot of air; the crumpled ball pushed against very little. In the absence of air resistance, all objects fall at the same rate regardless of mass. This constant downward acceleration due to gravity is approximately **9.8 m/s²** — meaning the object gains 9.8 meters per second of velocity every second it falls.

**Record:** Write your observation. Compare the crumpled paper drop to the flat paper drop.

> *You just isolated a variable. The paper's mass stayed the same but its shape changed. By controlling mass and changing shape, you identified air resistance as the factor that made the flat paper fall slower — not its mass.*

---

### Step 10. Set up the accelerometer app.

Open the accelerometer app on your phone. Find the setting that displays acceleration along the vertical axis (usually labeled "z" or "y" depending on phone orientation). Set the app to record data. Place the phone flat on a table and confirm the z-axis reads approximately 9.8 m/s² — this is the acceleration due to gravity acting on the phone while it sits still.

**Record:** Write the resting acceleration value from the app.

---

### Step 11. Measure the force of impact.

You will now use the phone to measure the acceleration experienced by each object when it strikes a surface. Place the phone on a firm pillow or a folded towel on the floor (this protects the phone). Drop the rubber ball from a height of 30 cm onto the phone. Check the accelerometer's peak reading.

**Observe:** The app records a spike in acceleration at the moment of impact — much higher than 9.8 m/s². This is the ***force*** of impact. In physics, force is defined precisely: ***force*** equals mass times acceleration. The unit of force is the ***Newton*** (N), named after Isaac Newton. One Newton is the force required to accelerate a 1 kg mass at 1 m/s².

**Record:** Write the peak acceleration from the impact. If you know the ball's mass, calculate the force: Force (N) = mass (kg) × acceleration (m/s²).

---

### Step 12. Compare light and heavy impacts.

Drop the rubber ball from 30 cm onto the phone again — record the peak acceleration. Now drop the small book from the same height onto the phone — record the peak acceleration.

**Observe:** The heavier book produces a higher force on impact, even though both fell the same distance. This is because force = mass × acceleration. If two objects experience similar acceleration (same drop height), the heavier one exerts more force on what it hits. This is why a bowling ball is dangerous to drop on your foot and a tennis ball is not — same acceleration, different mass, dramatically different force.

**Record:** Write both peak accelerations. Calculate the force (in Newtons) for each.

> *You just measured force quantitatively — as a product of mass and acceleration, not as a vague sense of "harder" or "softer."*

---

### Step 13. The push test: force and acceleration.

Place the rubber ball on a smooth, flat surface (table or hard floor). Flick it gently with one finger. Observe how fast it accelerates. Now find a heavier object of similar size (the full water bottle on its side, or a can of food). Flick it with the same force.

**Observe:** The ball accelerated much faster from the same flick. The water bottle barely moved. This is the most important relationship in physics: **F = ma**, rearranged as **a = F/m**. When the force is the same, a less massive object accelerates more. The resistance of an object to changes in its motion is called ***inertia***, and it is directly related to ***mass***. More mass means more inertia — more resistance to being pushed, pulled, stopped, or redirected.

**Record:** Describe what happened to each object. Write one sentence using the words force, mass, and acceleration.

---

### Step 14. Build a data table.

In your notebook, create a table with four columns: **Object**, **Mass (kg)**, **Drop Observation (simultaneous test)**, and **Impact Force (N)**. Fill in all rows using the data you collected.

**Observe:** Your data tells a clear story. During free fall, mass does not determine how fast an object falls — all objects accelerate at 9.8 m/s² (when air resistance is negligible). But mass absolutely determines the force of impact and the resistance to being pushed. Mass is the measure of how much matter an object contains and how much it resists acceleration.

**Record:** Complete the table.

## 4. Key Terminology

Five terms were introduced during this experiment. Each is anchored to a specific observation you made.

| Term | Definition | You Observed It When... |
|---|---|---|
| **Force** | A push or pull on an object, equal to mass times acceleration (F = ma). Measured in Newtons (N). | The accelerometer recorded a spike when the ball and book struck the phone — you calculated the force from mass × acceleration (Steps 11–12). |
| **Newton** | The SI unit of force. One Newton is the force required to accelerate a 1-kilogram mass at 1 meter per second squared (1 N = 1 kg·m/s²). | You calculated impact forces in Newtons from your drop tests (Steps 11–12). |
| **Acceleration** | The rate of change of velocity over time, measured in meters per second squared (m/s²). Objects in free fall near Earth accelerate at approximately 9.8 m/s². | The water bottle started at rest and sped up continuously as it fell — it was accelerating under gravity. The accelerometer confirmed ~9.8 m/s² at rest (Steps 4, 10). |
| **Inertia** | The tendency of an object to resist changes in its state of motion. An object at rest tends to stay at rest; an object in motion tends to stay in motion. Inertia is directly related to mass. | The heavy water bottle barely moved when you flicked it, while the light ball shot across the surface from the same force — the water bottle had more inertia (Step 13). |
| **Mass** | The amount of matter in an object, measured in kilograms. Mass determines both the gravitational force an object experiences and its resistance to acceleration (inertia). | You weighed three objects of different mass (Step 2) and found that mass determined impact force but not the rate of free fall (Steps 6–7, 11–12). |

## 5. Framework Connection

> You just tested one of the most famous claims in the history of science — the claim that all objects fall at the same rate regardless of mass. Galileo argued this in the early 1600s, contradicting nearly two thousand years of Aristotelian physics that said heavier objects fall faster. Your data supports Galileo: when air resistance is negligible, mass does not affect the rate of fall.
>
> The relationship you discovered between force, mass, and acceleration is Newton's Second Law of Motion: **F = ma**. It is the single most important equation in classical mechanics. Every time you push a door, throw a ball, brake a car, or launch a rocket, F = ma governs what happens. The force of gravity pulling an object toward Earth is its weight: W = mg, where g = 9.8 m/s². A 1 kg object experiences a gravitational force of 9.8 N. A 50 kg person experiences 490 N. The mass is different; the acceleration (g) is the same — which is exactly why they fall at the same rate.
>
> In Module P2, you will extend this: what happens when air resistance is *not* negligible? You will drop coffee filters with different numbers of stacked filters and discover terminal velocity — the point where the drag force exactly balances gravity and the object stops accelerating. That module begins where this one ends: with the realization that the flat sheet of paper was the clue all along.
