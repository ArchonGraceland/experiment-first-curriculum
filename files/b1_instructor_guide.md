## Setup Checklist (Before the Student Arrives)

- **Set up the microscope.** Turn it on, confirm the light source works. Start with the lowest power objective. Focus on an empty slide to make sure the optics are clean.
- **Clean the lenses.** Use lens paper only — never cloth or paper towels, which scratch optical coatings. If the image is hazy, the objective lens likely has oil or fingerprint residue.
- **Pre-cut the onion.** Cut it in half so the student can easily access the inner layers. Having the onion pre-cut saves 2–3 minutes and avoids the student struggling with a knife.
- **Lay out slides, coverslips, stains, toothpick, tweezers, dropper bottle of water, and gloves** on the workspace. Everything visible, nothing in drawers.
- **Test the stains yourself.** Put a drop of methylene blue on a slide and confirm it's the right concentration — it should be dark blue, not pale. Put a drop of iodine on a piece of onion skin and confirm you can see cell walls at 100×.
- **Have extra slides and coverslips ready.** First attempts at slide preparation often produce air bubbles or folded tissue. The student may need to redo one.
- **Make a cheek cell slide yourself before the session.** Confirm you can see clearly stained cells at 400×. If the cells are pale or sparse, you may need to scrape more firmly or use more stain.
- **Have the lab notebook worksheet and pen at the workspace.**

## Safety Notes

> **This is a low-risk experiment.**
>
> **Chemical safety:**
>
> - **Methylene blue stains skin and fabric.** Gloves are required when handling the stain. If it gets on skin, wash immediately — it will fade in hours. On clothing, the stain may be permanent.
> - **Iodine is mildly irritating to skin.** Gloves protect against this. If it contacts eyes, rinse with water for 5 minutes.
> - **Neither stain is dangerous in the quantities used** (single drops), but treat them with respect as part of building lab habits.
>
> **Glass safety:**
>
> - **Slides and coverslips have sharp edges.** Handle by the edges, not the flat surfaces.
> - **If a coverslip breaks, do not pick up fragments with bare fingers.** Use tweezers or a damp paper towel.
>
> **Biosafety:**
>
> - **The cheek cell scraping is the student's own tissue.** Dispose of the toothpick after use. Do not share toothpicks between people.
> - **Dispose of all used slides and coverslips in a designated container** — not in regular trash (glass hazard).
> - **Wash hands after the experiment.**

## Expected Results Reference

**Cheek cells (methylene blue, 400×):**

- **Shape:** Irregular, roughly round or oval. Flattened — these are squamous epithelial cells.
- **Size:** 60–80 μm across. At 400×, 3–5 cells should fit across the field of view.
- **Nucleus:** Dark blue, roughly circular, near the center of the cell. Clearly visible in well-stained cells.
- **Cytoplasm:** Lighter blue, granular appearance. Fills the area between the nucleus and the cell membrane.
- **Cell membrane:** Visible as the outer boundary of the cell — a thin line where the blue cytoplasm meets the clear background. Often hard to distinguish from the edge of the cytoplasm.
- **Arrangement:** Scattered, overlapping, no regular pattern. Cells often clump together.
- **Common issue:** Cells may appear very pale if insufficient stain was used, or as dark blobs if too much stain was used. One drop is usually right.

**Onion cells (iodine, 400×):**

- **Shape:** Rectangular or brick-like. Very regular.
- **Size:** 100–400 μm long, 25–50 μm wide. Significantly larger than cheek cells.
- **Cell wall:** Thick, clearly visible dark lines forming the rectangular grid. This is the most prominent feature.
- **Nucleus:** A small, darkly stained oval body, often pressed against one side of the cell rather than centered.
- **Cytoplasm:** Pale yellow-brown. Much of the cell interior may appear empty — this is the large central vacuole (not explicitly named in this module, but you can mention it if the student asks "Why does the cell look mostly empty?").
- **Arrangement:** Highly organized, brick-like rows. No overlap.

**If the student asks about the large empty space in onion cells:** This is the central vacuole, a membrane-bound compartment filled with water and dissolved substances. It maintains cell pressure (turgor) and is why plant cells look "mostly empty" compared to animal cells. You can name it if asked, but it's not a required term for this module.

## Timing Guide

| Time | Activity | Your Role |
|---|---|---|
| **0–2 min** | Read phenomenon hook, draw prediction sketch | Say nothing. Let them draw what they imagine. The prediction sketch is fun — most students draw a circle with a dot in the middle. |
| **2–5 min** | Prepare cheek cell slide (Steps 2–6) | Help with coverslip technique if needed — the 45-degree angle matters to avoid bubbles. |
| **5–10 min** | View cheek cells at low power (Step 7) | If they can't find cells, check: Is the slide on the stage correctly? Is the light on? Is the stain visible as a blue spot? Help focus at low power only. |
| **10–18 min** | View at high power, identify structures (Steps 8–9) | Ask: "Can you find the dark circle inside the cell? What do you think it is?" Let them look before naming anything. |
| **18–22 min** | Prepare onion cell slide (Steps 10–14) | The onion epidermis peel is the trickiest part. If the student can't get a thin enough layer, help with the tweezers — but let them try first. |
| **22–30 min** | View onion cells, identify structures (Steps 15–16) | The contrast with cheek cells should be striking. Ask: "How are these different from your cheek cells? What structure do you see here that you didn't see before?" |
| **30–35 min** | Organelles discussion, comparison table (Steps 17–18) | This is the synthesis moment. Ask: "What do both cell types have in common? What's different?" |
| **35–55 min** | Research report via digital tool | Available for terminology questions. Do not dictate. |

**If the student finishes slides quickly:** Encourage them to make a second cheek cell slide with more or less stain, or to try viewing the onion epidermis unstained first and then stained, to see the difference the stain makes.

**If the microscope is frustrating:** Microscopy has a learning curve. The most common problem is focusing: students at high power try to use the coarse knob and can't find the image. Demonstrate the coarse-to-fine focus procedure once, then let them practice.

## How to Facilitate Without Lecturing

### The Golden Rules (same as every module)

> - **Never explain before they observe.** If the student asks "What's a cell?" before they look through the microscope, say: "You're about to see one. Let's look first."
> - **Ask, don't tell.** Instead of "That dark circle is the nucleus," ask: "What's that dark circle inside the cell? What do you think it does?"
> - **Let them struggle for 30 seconds before helping.** If they can't find cells at 400×, they may need to re-center the slide or adjust the fine focus. Give them time.
> - **Celebrate the observation, not the answer.** "I see a brick pattern" is an excellent observation. "I see cells" comes next. Don't rush to the vocabulary.
> - **Never touch their notebook.**

### Module-Specific Facilitation Notes

**On the prediction sketch:** The student's initial drawing of "what a cell looks like" is a valuable artifact. Don't correct it before the experiment. After they've seen real cells, ask them to compare their prediction sketch to their observation drawing. The gap between prediction and reality is a learning moment.

**On the "gross" factor of cheek scraping:** Some students are uncomfortable scraping their mouth. Normalize it: "You're not cutting anything — you're just collecting cells that were about to fall off anyway. Your mouth lining replaces itself every 3–5 days." If the student truly refuses, an alternative is scraping the outer skin of the wrist (less effective but works with heavier staining).

**On the "I can't see anything" frustration:** This is the most common difficulty in microscopy. The solution is almost always: (1) confirm the stained area is actually under the objective, (2) increase the light, (3) start at low power and focus there before switching to high. If all else fails, look through the microscope yourself, find a good field of cells, and then let the student look without touching the focus knobs — so they know what they're looking for.

**On the mitochondria discussion (Step 17):** The student may be frustrated that they can't see mitochondria. This is actually a good teaching moment about instrument limitations. Ask: "Why do you think you can't see them? Are they not there, or are they too small for this microscope?" This plants the seed for understanding microscope resolution and the difference between light and electron microscopy.

## Common Mistakes and Troubleshooting

| Problem | Likely Cause | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| **No cells visible at any magnification** | Not enough cells on the slide; cells not in the stained area; focus is on the wrong layer | Make a new slide with a more vigorous cheek scrape. Add more stain. Start over at low power. |
| **Cells are dark blue blobs with no internal detail** | Too much stain | Make a new slide with one drop of stain (not two). Or let the current slide sit — sometimes stain diffuses over 5 minutes and contrast improves. |
| **Cells are barely visible / too pale** | Not enough stain | Add another drop of stain to the edge of the coverslip. Capillary action will pull it under. Wait 1 minute. |
| **Air bubbles trapped under coverslip** | Coverslip was placed too fast (dropped flat instead of lowered at an angle) | Make a new slide. Demonstrate the 45-degree technique. |
| **Onion epidermis is too thick** | Student peeled a layer of onion flesh, not just the thin epidermal skin | Show them: the epidermis is the nearly invisible translucent film on the inner surface of each fleshy layer. Use tweezers to peel from the concave side. |
| **Student can't focus at high power** | Using coarse focus knob instead of fine focus | Demonstrate: focus at low power first, center the target cells, THEN switch to high power and use ONLY the fine knob. |
| **Student conflates cell wall and cell membrane** | Easy confusion — both are boundaries | Anchor to the physical: "The cell wall is the thick line you can see. The cell membrane is too thin to see clearly at this magnification — it's just inside the wall. Your cheek cells have a membrane but NO wall. That's why they're squishy and round instead of rigid and rectangular." |

## What to Watch For

### Signs of Understanding

- The student's 400× drawings clearly show a nucleus, cytoplasm, and membrane/wall — and these are labeled correctly.
- The student can explain the plant/animal cell difference using "cell wall" without prompting.
- The student spontaneously asks why plant cells are rectangular: "Is it because of the wall?"
- The student connects the cell comparison to something real: "Is that why wood is hard and skin is soft?"
- The research report comparison table is filled out with accurate observations.

### Signs of Difficulty

- The student's drawings look the same for cheek and onion cells — they haven't noticed the structural differences.
- The student cannot identify the nucleus in a well-stained cell.
- The student confuses the cell wall with the cell membrane.
- The notebook has few entries or no drawings.

### If Difficulty Persists

Go back to the microscope. Place both slides side by side. Have the student look at one, then the other, and narrate what they see aloud. Ask: "What shape are the cheek cells? What shape are the onion cells? Why are the shapes different?" The answer is right there in the lens — they just need to articulate it.

## Research Report Coaching Reference

**Do not hand these to the student or read them aloud as answers.** Use them to ask guiding questions.

### Section 1: Research Question — What a Strong One Looks Like

> *"How do the structures of human cheek cells (animal cells) compare to onion epidermis cells (plant cells) when observed under a compound light microscope?"*

**What makes this strong:** Specifies both cell types, identifies them as animal vs. plant, and names the tool.

### Section 2: Hypothesis — What a Strong One Looks Like

> *"I hypothesize that both cell types will have a nucleus and cell membrane because these are fundamental structures of all living cells, but the plant cell may have additional structures that the animal cell lacks, because plants and animals have very different physical properties (rigid vs. flexible)."*

### Section 3: Experimental Design — What a Strong One Looks Like

> *"I prepared two wet-mount microscope slides: one with human cheek cells stained with methylene blue, and one with onion epidermis stained with iodine. I viewed both at 100× and 400× magnification. The stains increased contrast so I could see internal structures."*

### Section 4: Results — What a Strong One Looks Like

> *"Cheek cells were round or oval, ~70 μm across, scattered and overlapping. Each had a clearly visible dark blue nucleus near the center, lighter blue cytoplasm, and a faint cell membrane at the edge. Onion cells were rectangular, ~200 μm long, arranged in rigid brick-like rows. Each had a thick cell wall, a small off-center nucleus, and mostly clear cytoplasm with a large empty-looking interior. Neither cell type showed visible mitochondria at 400×."*

### Section 5: Analysis & Conclusion — What a Strong One Looks Like

> *"Both cell types share fundamental structures: a cell membrane, cytoplasm, a nucleus, and organelles including mitochondria (present but too small to see). The key difference is the cell wall — a rigid cellulose structure present in the onion cell but absent in the cheek cell. The cell wall explains why plant cells are rectangular and rigidly arranged while animal cells are round and flexible. This structural difference is why plant tissue (like a celery stalk) is rigid without internal bones, while animal tissue requires a skeleton for support."*

### Section 6: Next Investigation — What a Strong One Looks Like

> *"I want to compare a third cell type. I would observe a leaf cell (such as elodea) under the microscope to see if I can find chloroplasts — the organelles responsible for photosynthesis. I predict the leaf cell will have a cell wall like the onion but will also contain green structures (chloroplasts) that the onion cell lacks, because onion cells are underground and do not perform photosynthesis."*

## Research Report Review Rubric

| Dimension | Strong | Needs Work |
|---|---|---|
| **Vocabulary Precision** | Uses 5–6 key terms correctly. "The nucleus was visible in both cell types, but the onion cell also had a rigid cell wall." | Uses colloquial substitutions: "the plant cell had a thick edge." Feedback: "What's the scientific name for that thick outer boundary?" |
| **Evidence-Based Reasoning** | Cites specific observations: "The cheek cells were round (~70 μm) while the onion cells were rectangular (~200 μm), explained by the presence of the cell wall." | Makes claims without observations: "Plant and animal cells are different." Feedback: "How exactly were they different? What did you see?" |
| **Investigative Thinking** | Proposes a specific follow-up — observing a different cell type, testing a different stain, or looking for chloroplasts. | Vague: "I'd look at more cells." Feedback: "Which cells? What would you expect to see differently?" |

**Remember:** A Phase 1 report that uses 4 of 6 terms correctly is a success. Microscopy is difficult for beginners, and accurate observation under a microscope is a new skill.

## Content Knowledge Reference

**Cell theory (the three tenets):**

1. All living organisms are composed of one or more cells.
2. The cell is the basic unit of structure and function in living organisms.
3. All cells arise from pre-existing cells.

This module addresses the first two. The third is addressed in B5 (Cell Division).

**Cheek cells (squamous epithelial cells):**

These are flat, scale-like cells from the stratified squamous epithelium lining the oral cavity. They are constantly shed and replaced (turnover: 3–5 days). Each contains a complete diploid genome (46 chromosomes). Methylene blue preferentially stains nucleic acids (DNA and RNA), which is why the nucleus appears darkest.

**Onion epidermis cells:**

These are non-photosynthetic plant cells from the outer skin of the onion bulb. They have a cellulose cell wall (the thick grid lines), a cell membrane just inside the wall, a large central vacuole (the "empty" interior — filled with water and dissolved solutes), and a nucleus typically displaced to one edge by the vacuole. Iodine stains starch and some proteins, providing contrast for the wall and nucleus.

**Why can't you see mitochondria?**

Mitochondria are 0.5–1.0 μm in diameter. The resolution limit of a standard light microscope is ~0.2 μm, so mitochondria are near the theoretical limit. In practice, they require specific stains (like Janus Green B) or fluorescent dyes to be visible. At 400×, they are below the practical resolution of most student microscopes.

**Connection to AP Biology**

Aligns with AP Biology Unit 2 (Cell Structure and Function). Cell components, plant vs. animal cells, and organelle function are directly assessed in Learning Objectives 2.1–2.10.

## Cleanup

- Dispose of the used toothpick in a waste container (not reusable — biosafety).
- Dispose of used slides and coverslips in a designated glass waste container, or wrap in several layers of paper towel before putting in trash.
- Wipe down the workspace — methylene blue and iodine stains will mark surfaces.
- Clean the microscope objectives with lens paper. Return the microscope to lowest power and turn off the light.
- Dispose of the onion piece (or save the other half for cooking — the student's half is stained).
- Remove gloves, wash hands.
