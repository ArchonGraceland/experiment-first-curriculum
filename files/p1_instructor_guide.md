## Setup Checklist (Before the Student Arrives)

- **Install the accelerometer app on the student's phone.** Test it — confirm it displays acceleration in m/s² and that the z-axis reads ~9.8 m/s² when the phone is flat on a table. phyphox and Physics Toolbox Sensor Suite are both free and reliable.
- **Assemble the three drop objects.** Full water bottle (~500 g), a small hardback book (~300 g), and an empty water bottle (~30 g). Weigh each and confirm there is a clear mass difference.
- **Find two hardback books of noticeably different mass** for the simultaneous drop test. A thick textbook and a thin paperback work well.
- **Have a sheet of standard paper ready.**
- **Mark the drop height.** Measure 1.5 m from the floor on a wall or door frame. Place a small piece of tape at that height so the student has a consistent reference.
- **Identify the drop zone.** Hard floor (tile, wood, concrete). Clear the area of anything breakable. If working outside, flat concrete is ideal.
- **Set up the phone impact station.** Place a firm pillow or folded towel on the floor where the student will drop the rubber ball and book onto the phone. Test it: drop the ball from 30 cm, confirm the accelerometer registers a clear spike.
- **Have the lab notebook worksheet and pen at the workspace.**

---

## Safety Notes

> **This is a low-risk experiment.** The objects being dropped are household items at low heights.
>
> **However:**
>
> - **Goggles are required.** Building the habit for later modules.
> - **Keep feet clear of the drop zone.** A full water bottle from 1.5 m can bruise a foot.
> - **Do not drop heavy objects onto the phone.** The phone impact test uses only the rubber ball (30 cm height) and a light book (30 cm height). The pillow/towel protects against reasonable impacts. If the student wants to drop heavier objects on the phone, redirect them.
> - **The phone is the most expensive piece of equipment in this experiment.** Ensure the pillow/towel cushion is adequate before the first impact test.
> - **If working outdoors:** Wind will affect the flat paper test (Step 8). This is actually fine — it reinforces the air resistance concept. But if wind is strong, do Steps 8–9 indoors.

## Expected Results Reference

**Simultaneous drop tests:**

| Test | Expected Result | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Full bottle vs. empty bottle | Hit at same time (±0.05 s) | The key result — mass doesn't affect free fall rate |
| Heavy book vs. light book | Hit at same time | Replication with different objects |
| Book vs. flat paper | Book hits first, significantly | Air resistance dominates the paper's fall |
| Book vs. crumpled paper | Hit at nearly same time | Crumpling removes air resistance, revealing equal free fall |

**Accelerometer readings:**

- **Phone at rest (flat on table):** ~9.8 m/s² on the vertical axis. This is gravitational acceleration.
- **Rubber ball impact (30 cm drop):** Expect a peak of 20–80 m/s² depending on ball hardness, surface, and phone sensitivity.
- **Book impact (30 cm drop):** Expect a peak of 30–120 m/s² — higher than the ball because the book doesn't bounce.

**Force calculations:**

- If rubber ball mass = 0.06 kg and peak acceleration = 50 m/s²: F = 0.06 × 50 = 3.0 N
- If book mass = 0.3 kg and peak acceleration = 60 m/s²: F = 0.3 × 60 = 18.0 N
- The key point: heavier object exerts dramatically more force from the same drop height.

**The push test (Step 13):**

- Light ball: flicked with one finger, it shoots across the surface.
- Heavy bottle: flicked with the same finger, it barely moves.
- Same force, different mass → different acceleration. This is F = ma in action.

## Timing Guide

| Time | Activity | Your Role |
|---|---|---|
| **0–2 min** | Read phenomenon hook, write prediction | Say nothing. Let them read and write. Almost everyone predicts the heavy object falls faster. |
| **2–5 min** | Weigh objects, mark drop height (Steps 2–3) | Only help if the scale needs calibrating. |
| **5–8 min** | Drop heavy object, observe (Step 4) | Ask: "Did it seem to speed up or fall at a constant speed?" |
| **8–10 min** | Drop light object (Step 5) | Don't comment yet on timing comparison. |
| **10–13 min** | Simultaneous drop — the key moment (Step 6) | This is the surprise. If they hit at the same time and the student is shocked, let them sit with it. Ask: "Want to try it again?" |
| **13–15 min** | Two-book replication (Step 7) | Ask: "Same result?" |
| **15–18 min** | Book vs. flat paper (Step 8) | The student will say "But the paper is lighter!" This is the setup for Step 9. |
| **18–21 min** | Crumpled paper test (Step 9) | This is the second aha moment. Ask: "The paper weighs the same as before. What changed?" |

| **30–35 min** | Push test, data table (Steps 13–14) | Ask: "If you push a shopping cart and a truck with the same force, which accelerates more? Why?" |

## How to Facilitate Without Lecturing

### The Golden Rules (same as every module)

> - **Never explain before they observe.** If the student asks "Won't the heavy one fall faster?" before Step 6, say: "Let's find out."
> - **Ask, don't tell.** Instead of "All objects fall at the same rate," ask: "What did you hear when you dropped them? Same time or different?"
> - **Let them struggle for 30 seconds before helping.** If the accelerometer app is confusing, wait. They'll likely figure out which axis shows the spike.
> - **Celebrate the data, not the "right answer."** If the drops weren't perfectly simultaneous, that's data. Ask: "Which one hit first? By how much? What might have caused that?"
> - **Never touch their notebook.**

### Module-Specific Facilitation Notes

**On the prediction:** Almost every student (and most adults) predicts the heavier object falls faster. This is the point — the counterintuitive result is the engagement engine. When the student discovers they were wrong, resist the urge to explain why. Let them sit with the confusion. The crumpled paper test (Step 9) is where the resolution happens.

**On the flat paper "contradiction" (Steps 8–9):** The student will likely say "See, the paper IS slower because it's lighter." Do not correct this. Instead, say: "Okay — then crumple the paper into a ball and try again." When the crumpled paper falls at the same rate as the book, the student will realize the mass didn't change — only the shape did. This is the variable isolation moment, and it's much more powerful if the student discovers it than if you explain it.

**On F = ma:** This equation may feel abstract to a student encountering it for the first time. Keep anchoring it to the physical: "You flicked the ball and it flew. You flicked the bottle and it barely moved. Same flick — same force. Different mass — different acceleration. That IS F = ma."

**On the accelerometer:** The app can be noisy and confusing. If the student is overwhelmed by the data display, simplify: "Just watch the number. Drop the ball. What was the biggest number you saw?" The precise value matters less than the comparison between the ball and the book.

## Common Mistakes and Troubleshooting

| Problem | Likely Cause | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| **Heavy object seems to hit first** | Student released one hand slightly before the other | Practice releasing simultaneously. Use the "open both hands at the same time" technique. Try a few times. |
| **Objects clearly don't hit at the same time** | Very different shapes (e.g., one is flat, one is round) creating different air resistance | Use objects of similar shape and size, differing primarily in mass. Two books of different thickness work well. |
| **Accelerometer reads 0 at rest** | Wrong axis selected, or phone orientation doesn't match app expectations | Flip the phone. Try a different axis (x, y, z). In phyphox, use "Acceleration (without g)" or "Acceleration (with g)" and see which shows ~9.8 at rest. |
| **No clear spike during impact test** | Phone cushion is too soft; ball bouncing absorbs the impact | Use a firmer surface under the phone (thin towel, not a pillow). Or place the phone screen-down on the ball landing zone. |
| **Student conflates mass and weight** | Very common — "mass" and "weight" are used interchangeably in everyday language | This is fine for now. Say: "Mass is how much stuff is in the object. Weight is the force of gravity pulling on that stuff. In this experiment, we're measuring mass in kilograms and calculating force in Newtons." |
| **Student doesn't understand why F = ma matters** | The equation feels abstract | Anchor to the push test: "Same push (force), heavy object barely moves (low acceleration), light object shoots across (high acceleration). The equation F = ma is just that observation written in math." |

## What to Watch For

### Signs of Understanding

- The student is genuinely surprised that the heavy and light objects hit at the same time — and can articulate why by the end of the module.
- The student explains the flat paper result using air resistance, not mass.
- The student uses "force," "mass," and "acceleration" correctly in conversation.
- The student spontaneously applies F = ma to a new situation: "So a car is harder to push because it has more mass."
- The research report analysis cites specific accelerometer readings or calculated forces.

### Signs of Difficulty

- The student still believes the heavy object fell faster, even after multiple simultaneous drops.
- The student cannot explain why the crumpled paper fell differently from the flat paper.
- The student confuses force and mass: "The heavy one has more force."
- The notebook has few entries, especially during the accelerometer steps.

### If Difficulty Persists

Go back to the bench. Have the student hold the crumpled paper in one hand and the flat paper in the other. Ask: "Which one is heavier?" (Neither — same mass.) "Drop both." (Crumpled one falls faster.) "The mass is exactly the same. So mass isn't why the flat one fell slowly. What's different?" Let them articulate that shape/air resistance is the variable.

## Research Report Coaching Reference

**Do not hand these to the student or read them aloud as answers.** Use them to ask guiding questions.

### Section 1: Research Question — What a Strong One Looks Like

> *"Does the mass of an object affect how fast it falls when dropped from the same height?"*

**What makes this strong:** Names the independent variable (mass) and dependent variable (rate of fall), with the controlled variable implied (same height). If the student writes "Does heavy stuff fall faster?" ask: "What specifically are you changing? What are you measuring?"

### Section 2: Hypothesis — What a Strong One Looks Like

> *"I hypothesize that the heavier object will hit the ground first because a greater mass experiences a greater gravitational force (F = mg), which should accelerate it faster. However, I'm uncertain because I've heard that all objects fall at the same rate in a vacuum."*

**Note:** An incorrect prediction with sound reasoning is perfectly valid as a hypothesis. The Analysis section is where the student addresses whether the data supported or contradicted it.

### Section 3: Experimental Design — What a Strong One Looks Like

> *"I dropped objects of different mass (full water bottle at 0.5 kg, empty bottle at 0.03 kg) from the same height (1.5 m) simultaneously. My independent variable was the mass of the object. My dependent variable was whether the objects hit the ground at the same time. I controlled the drop height, release method (clean release, no push), and drop location (same spot, indoors, no wind)."*

### Section 4: Results — What a Strong One Looks Like

> *"The full water bottle (0.50 kg) and empty bottle (0.03 kg) hit the ground at the same time when dropped simultaneously from 1.5 m. The two books (0.80 kg and 0.25 kg) also hit at the same time. The flat sheet of paper took approximately 3 seconds longer than the book to reach the ground. The crumpled paper hit at the same time as the book. The accelerometer read 9.8 m/s² at rest. The rubber ball (0.06 kg) registered a peak of 45 m/s² on impact (F = 2.7 N). The book (0.30 kg) registered 62 m/s² (F = 18.6 N)."*

### Section 5: Analysis & Conclusion — What a Strong One Looks Like

> *"My hypothesis was contradicted: the heavier object did not fall faster. Objects of different mass hit the ground at the same time when dropped from the same height, because gravitational acceleration is constant (9.8 m/s²) regardless of mass. The flat paper appeared to contradict this, but the crumpled paper test proved that air resistance — not mass — caused the difference, since crumpling changed the shape but not the mass. However, mass does determine the force of impact: the 0.30 kg book exerted 18.6 N while the 0.06 kg ball exerted only 2.7 N, consistent with F = ma. Mass also determines inertia — the heavy water bottle resisted the flick force far more than the light ball."*

### Section 6: Next Investigation — What a Strong One Looks Like

> *"I want to investigate how drop height affects impact force. I would drop the same ball from 10 cm, 30 cm, 60 cm, and 100 cm onto the accelerometer, recording peak acceleration at each height. I predict the impact force will increase with height because the object has more time to accelerate and reaches a higher velocity before impact."*

## Research Report Review Rubric

| Dimension | Strong | Needs Work |
|---|---|---|
| **Vocabulary Precision** | Uses 4–5 key terms correctly. "The gravitational acceleration of 9.8 m/s² is independent of mass." | Uses colloquial substitutions: "the heavy one hits harder" instead of "exerts more force." Feedback: "What's the precise term for how hard it hits?" |
| **Evidence-Based Reasoning** | Cites specific data: "Both objects hit simultaneously from 1.5 m despite a 16× mass difference." | Makes claims without data: "They fell the same." Feedback: "What were the masses? What exactly did you observe?" |
| **Investigative Thinking** | Proposes a specific follow-up with named variables. | Vague: "I'd drop more stuff." Feedback: "What would you change? What would you measure?" |

**Remember:** A Phase 1 report that uses 3 of 5 terms correctly is a success. Calibrate expectations accordingly.

## Content Knowledge Reference

**Why do all objects fall at the same rate?**

The gravitational force on an object is F = mg (weight). Newton's Second Law says F = ma, so ma = mg, and a = g. The mass cancels out. The acceleration due to gravity (g ≈ 9.8 m/s²) is independent of mass. This is true only in the absence of other forces (like air resistance). In a vacuum, a feather and a bowling ball fall at exactly the same rate — demonstrated by Apollo 15 astronaut David Scott on the Moon in 1971.

**What about air resistance?**

Air resistance (drag) depends on the object's shape, surface area, and velocity — but not directly on its mass. A flat sheet of paper has a large surface area relative to its mass, so drag is significant compared to the gravitational force. A crumpled paper ball has much less surface area, so drag is negligible and it falls like any other compact object.

**Newton's Three Laws of Motion (for reference):**

1. An object at rest stays at rest, and an object in motion stays in motion at constant velocity, unless acted upon by a net force (inertia).
2. F = ma. Force equals mass times acceleration.
3. For every action, there is an equal and opposite reaction.

This module primarily introduces Newton's Second Law and touches on the First Law (inertia). The Third Law appears in P11 (Momentum and Collisions).

**Connection to AP Physics**

Aligns with AP Physics 1 Unit 2 (Dynamics) and Unit 1 (Kinematics). Newton's Second Law is the foundational equation for AP Physics 1 and appears in nearly every unit. Free fall under gravity connects to Unit 1 Learning Objectives.
