## 1. Phenomenon Hook

> A lemon, a nail, a strip of copper wire, and a voltmeter. The needle moves. There is no battery in this circuit — so where is the electricity coming from?

## 3. The Experiment

**Safety first:** Put on safety goggles. The voltages in this experiment are far too low to feel, but goggles are standard practice whenever you work with metal strips and acidic solutions. Protect your work surface with newspaper or a tray.

Open your lab notebook to a fresh page and fill in the date and module number before you begin.

---

### Step 1. Prepare the lemon cell.

Roll the lemon firmly on the table with your palm, pressing down to break the internal membranes and release juice. Insert a zinc strip and a copper strip into the lemon, approximately 3 cm apart. Push each strip about halfway in. The strips must not touch each other inside the fruit.

**Record:** In your lab notebook, sketch the setup. Label the two strips.

> **METHOD SIDEBAR**
> *You just performed a key scientific skill: controlling your setup. By keeping the strips the same depth, the same distance apart, and ensuring they don't touch, you created a controlled experiment. This is called establishing controlled variables.*

---

### Step 2. Measure the voltage.

Set the first multimeter to DC voltage (the V with a straight line above it). Touch the red probe to the copper strip and the black probe to the zinc strip. Read the display.

**What to observe:** The multimeter shows a positive voltage. This means electrical potential exists between the two metals. That potential difference is called ***voltage***: the measure of how much energy is available to push electrons through a circuit, measured in volts (V).

**Record:** Write the exact voltage reading to two decimal places in your notebook.

> **METHOD SIDEBAR**
> *You just made a quantitative observation — a measurement expressed as a number with units, not just "the meter moved." Quantitative observations are the foundation of scientific evidence.*

---

### Step 3. Examine the zinc strip.

Carefully pull the zinc strip out of the lemon and look closely at the surface that was inside the fruit. Compare it to the end that stayed outside.

**What to observe:** Look at the submerged portion of the zinc strip. Does the surface look different from the portion that stayed outside the fruit? Here is what is happening at the atomic level: zinc atoms are each losing two electrons and entering the citric acid solution as zinc ions (Zn²⁺). This process — the loss of electrons — is called ***oxidation***. The zinc strip is the ***electrode*** where oxidation occurs, which makes it the anode.

**Record:** Describe what the zinc surface looks like. Sketch the difference between the submerged and exposed portions.

> **METHOD SIDEBAR**
> *You just practiced a critical scientific move: connecting a visible observation (changes on the metal surface) to an invisible mechanism (atoms losing electrons). This is the bridge between observation and theory.*

---

### Step 4. Think about the copper.

If the zinc is losing electrons, where are those electrons going? They flow through the external circuit (the wires and multimeter) toward the copper strip. At the copper surface, hydrogen ions (H⁺) from the citric acid in the lemon juice gain those electrons and become hydrogen gas (H₂). This process — the gain of electrons — is called ***reduction***. The copper strip is the electrode where reduction occurs, which makes it the cathode.

**What to observe:** Look very closely at the copper strip inside the lemon. Can you see anything forming on its surface? If so, that is hydrogen gas, the product of reduction.

**Record:** Note whether you see bubbles on the copper. Record the observation either way.

> **METHOD SIDEBAR**
> *You just connected two observations into a complete mechanism: oxidation at one electrode, reduction at the other. Together, this pair of reactions is called a redox reaction. Science often works this way — separate observations that snap together into a single explanation.*

---

### Step 5. Identify the electrolyte.

The citric acid solution inside the lemon is doing essential work: it contains ions (charged particles) that carry charge between the two electrodes inside the fruit, completing the internal circuit. A substance that produces ions when dissolved, allowing a solution to conduct electricity, is called an ***electrolyte***. Without the electrolyte, no current flows — the electrodes would just be two pieces of metal sitting in pulp.

**Quick test:** Remove both strips from the lemon and press them together without fruit between them. Read the multimeter. What happened to the voltage?

**Record:** Write the voltage reading when the strips touch directly (no fruit). Compare to your lemon reading.

> **METHOD SIDEBAR**
> *You just performed what scientists call a negative control — removing one variable (the electrolyte) to confirm it is necessary for the result. If the voltage disappears without the lemon, the lemon is essential.*

---

### Step 6. Test different fruits.

Repeat Steps 1–2 with the potato, the apple, and the orange. Use fresh zinc and copper strips for each fruit (or sand the used ones clean). Insert to the same depth, the same distance apart.

**What to observe:** Does each fruit produce the same voltage, or different? The voltage depends on the electrolyte — different fruits have different acid concentrations and different ion compositions.

**Record:** Create a table in your notebook with columns for fruit name, voltage reading, and any observations about electrode appearance. Record all four readings.

> **METHOD SIDEBAR**
> *You just designed a comparative experiment by changing one variable (the fruit) while holding everything else constant (electrode material, size, spacing, depth). The variable you change is the independent variable. The variable you measure is the dependent variable.*

---

### Step 7. Wire two lemon cells in series and light the LED.

Set up two fresh lemon cells. Connect the copper strip of the first cell to the zinc strip of the second cell using an alligator clip lead. Now connect the remaining zinc strip (Cell 1) to the short leg of the LED, and the remaining copper strip (Cell 2) to the long leg of the LED.

**What to observe:** What happens to the LED? Measure the combined voltage with your second multimeter. How does it compare to the voltage of a single cell? Chemical energy in the fruit is converting to electrical energy, which is converting to light energy.

**Record:** Write the voltage across both cells combined. Compare it to the sum of the individual cell voltages. Sketch the complete circuit, labeling all components.

> **METHOD SIDEBAR**
> *You just applied your findings to a practical problem: a single cell didn't have enough voltage, so you combined cells to increase it. This is engineering — using scientific understanding to design a solution.*

---

### Step 8. Measure the current.

Set the second multimeter to DC milliamps (mA). Disconnect one alligator clip from the LED circuit and insert the second multimeter in series (so current flows through the meter to reach the LED). Read the current.

**What to observe:** The current is very small. This is why the LED may glow dimly. The fruit battery has high internal resistance: the electrolyte is weak, and the electrode surface area is small. Voltage tells you the "push"; current tells you how many electrons are actually flowing per second.

**Record:** Write the current in milliamps. Note the LED brightness qualitatively (dim, moderate, barely visible).

> **METHOD SIDEBAR**
> *You just measured two different properties of the same circuit: voltage (the driving force) and current (the actual flow). Distinguishing between these two quantities is a fundamental skill in physics and chemistry.*

## 4. Key Terminology

Five terms were introduced during this experiment. Each is anchored to a specific observation you made.

| Term | Definition | You Observed It When... |
|---|---|---|
| **Voltage** | The electrical potential difference between two points, measured in volts. It represents the energy available to push electrons through a circuit. | The multimeter displayed a reading between the zinc and copper strips in the lemon (Step 2). |
| **Oxidation** | The loss of electrons by an atom, ion, or molecule during a chemical reaction. | The zinc strip showed surface changes after sitting in the lemon — zinc atoms lost electrons and entered the solution as Zn²⁺ ions (Step 3). |
| **Reduction** | The gain of electrons by an atom, ion, or molecule during a chemical reaction. | Tiny bubbles may have appeared on the copper strip inside the lemon — hydrogen ions gained electrons and became hydrogen gas (Step 4). |
| **Electrolyte** | A substance that produces ions when dissolved, allowing the solution to conduct electricity. | When you removed the fruit and pressed the metal strips together, the voltage dropped. The citric acid solution was conducting ions between the electrodes (Step 5). |
| **Electrode** | A conductor through which electricity enters or leaves a solution or substance. The anode is where oxidation occurs; the cathode is where reduction occurs. | The zinc strip (anode) and the copper strip (cathode) were the two electrodes in each fruit cell. Without both, no voltage was produced (Steps 2–4). |

## 5. Challenge Mode

> **THE ELECTRODE SWAP CHALLENGE**
>
> You measured four fruits. But the voltage depends on more than just the electrolyte — it depends on the difference in reactivity between the two electrode metals. What happens if you change the metals?

### Predict First

Before you start, write down your prediction in your lab notebook:

- *If you replace the copper strip with an iron nail (while keeping the zinc), will the voltage increase, decrease, or stay the same? Why?*
- *If you use two strips of the same metal (e.g., two zinc strips), what voltage do you predict? Why?*

### Then Test

1. Set up a lemon cell with zinc and iron electrodes. Measure and record the voltage.
2. Set up a lemon cell with two zinc electrodes (same metal). Measure and record the voltage.
3. Set up a lemon cell with two copper electrodes (same metal). Measure and record the voltage.
4. If you have other metals available (aluminum foil, a steel paperclip, a brass key), test as many combinations as you can. Record every reading.

### Analyze

Compare your predictions to your results. What pattern do you see? What determines how much voltage a cell produces?

## 6. Framework Connection

> The fruit battery you built is a galvanic cell — the simplest form of an electrochemical cell, and the operating principle behind every commercial battery from a AA cell to a lithium-ion laptop battery. The voltage you measured depends on the standard electrode potentials of the two metals, which are tabulated values that quantify how strongly each metal tends to lose or gain electrons. In Module AC2, you will reverse this process: instead of a spontaneous redox reaction producing electricity, you will use electricity to force a non-spontaneous redox reaction — electroplating metal onto a surface. That reversal is the difference between a galvanic cell and an electrolytic cell, and it is the foundation of industrial electrochemistry.
