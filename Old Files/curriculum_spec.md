# EXPERIMENT-FIRST SCIENCE CURRICULUM

## Curriculum Design Specification

**Target Learner:** 16-year-old with ADHD
**Scope:** High School Physics, Chemistry, Biology, Earth & Space Science
**Version:** 0.1 — Draft for Refinement
**Date:** March 2026

---

## 1. Design Philosophy

This curriculum inverts the traditional sequence. Instead of definition → explanation → example → lab, every unit follows the pattern:

> **Phenomenon → Experiment → Observation → Terminology → Framework**
>
> *The student encounters the science before they encounter the word for it.*

### 1.1 Core Principles

1. **Experiment-first, always.** No concept is introduced until the student has physically observed the phenomenon it describes. Vocabulary arrives at the moment of relevance, not before.

2. **Precise scientific language, never simplified.** Use the correct term from the first encounter. It is "oxidation," not "the metal breaking down." It is "homeostasis," not "staying the same." The term is taught through the experience, not replaced with a colloquial approximation.

3. **Short cycles with immediate payoff.** Each module is completable in 25–40 minutes. The observation-to-understanding arc closes within a single session. No multi-day buildups before the student sees a result.

4. **Interleaved subjects.** Physics, chemistry, and biology rotate rather than running in isolated blocks. This combats habituation and builds cross-disciplinary pattern recognition.

5. **The scientific method is taught by doing, not by lecture.** Methodological concepts (hypothesis formulation, variable isolation, controlled experimentation) are introduced through embedded scaffolding within experiments, not as a prerequisite unit.

6. **Counterintuitive results are the hook.** Every experiment should produce at least one result that surprises, contradicts intuition, or raises a new question. The cognitive dissonance is the engine of engagement.

## 2. Module Anatomy

Every module follows an identical structure. This predictability is deliberate — for an ADHD learner, knowing what comes next reduces executive function overhead and lets cognitive resources go toward the science itself.

### 2.1 Module Template

| Component | Description |
|---|---|
| **Module Title** | Framed as a question (e.g., "Can you generate electricity from fruit?") |
| **Phenomenon Hook** | A 1–2 sentence scenario, image, or demonstration that creates curiosity or cognitive dissonance. No terminology yet. |
| **Materials List** | Everything needed, with common household substitutions noted. Target: items available at a grocery store or online for under $20. |
| **The Experiment** | Step-by-step procedure. Written in imperative voice. Each step includes what to observe and what to record. Precise scientific terminology is introduced at the moment of observation. |
| **Method Sidebar** | Runs parallel to the experiment. Names the methodological step the student is performing (e.g., "You just formulated a hypothesis"). See Section 4 for the scaffolding progression. |
| **Key Terminology** | 3–5 terms introduced during this module, each anchored to a specific observation. Format: Term — definition — "see step N." |
| **Challenge Mode** | An extension question or modification to the experiment. Student predicts the outcome, then tests it. Designed to extend engagement for students who finish early or want more. |
| **Framework Connection** | A brief (2–3 sentence) explanation of how this experiment connects to the larger scientific framework. This is the only section that provides "textbook" context, and it comes last. |
| **Glossary Entry** | Pre-formatted entry for the student's living glossary (see Section 5). |

### 2.2 Constraints

- **Time:** 25–40 minutes for the core experiment. Challenge Mode may add 10–15 minutes.
- **Terminology:** 3–5 new terms per module. No more. Cognitive load must be managed.
- **Materials cost:** Under $20 per module, with most modules under $10. Common household items preferred.
- **Safety:** Every module includes a safety note where relevant. Adult supervision requirements are flagged explicitly.
- **Prerequisite transparency:** Every module lists which prior modules (if any) introduce terminology or concepts it builds on. No hidden dependencies.

## 3. Language Policy

This section exists because it is the single most important design decision in this curriculum. The language policy affects every sentence of every module.

### 3.1 The Rule

> **Always use the precise scientific term. Introduce it at the exact moment the student can observe what it refers to. Never substitute a colloquial approximation.**

### 3.2 How It Works in Practice

**Correct approach:**

*"See how the zinc strip is dissolving into the citric acid solution? That process is called **oxidation** — the zinc atoms are losing electrons. The term originally described reactions with oxygen, but in chemistry it refers to any loss of electrons. Write it down: **oxidation = loss of electrons.**"*

**Incorrect approach:**

*"See how the zinc is breaking down? That's because it's reacting with the acid. Later you'll learn this is called oxidation."*

The second version defers the terminology, which means the student now has a colloquial mental model ("breaking down") that will need to be overwritten later. The first version anchors the correct term to a physical observation in real time.

### 3.3 Terminology Accumulation

Terms introduced in one module are used without re-explanation in subsequent modules. The living glossary (Section 5) serves as the reference. If a student encounters a term they have forgotten, they look it up in their own glossary — which is anchored to their own experimental experience, not an abstract definition.

Expected accumulation rate: approximately 3–5 terms per module, yielding roughly 185–310 scientific terms across the full 62-module curriculum. This exceeds the vocabulary load of a standard high school science sequence, with significantly stronger retention due to experiential anchoring.

## 4. Scientific Method Scaffolding

The scientific method is not taught as a prerequisite. It is taught as a set of recurring practices that the student performs, names, and gradually takes ownership of across the full curriculum.

### 4.1 The Scaffolding Model

Each experiment includes a Method Sidebar that runs alongside the procedure. The sidebar names the methodological step the student is performing. Over time, the sidebar transitions from telling the student what they did to asking them to identify it themselves.

### 4.2 Progression Phases

| Phase | Modules | Sidebar Behavior | Student Responsibility |
|---|---|---|---|
| **1: Narrated** | 1–15 | Sidebar explicitly names each methodological step after the student performs it. "You just formulated a hypothesis — a testable prediction based on prior observation." | Follow the procedure. Read the sidebar. Begin recognizing the pattern. |
| **2: Prompted** | 16–30 | Sidebar asks the student to name the step before revealing the answer. "What did you just do? (Flip to see: You identified the independent variable.)" | Attempt to name the methodological step. Self-correct against the sidebar. |
| **3: Independent** | 31–48 | Sidebar provides only the category. "Methodological step: ______." Student fills in from memory. | Identify the step by name. Record it in the lab notebook. |
| **4: Designer** | 49–62 | No sidebar. Student designs the experimental procedure themselves, including controls, variables, and hypothesis. Module provides only the phenomenon and materials. | Full experimental design. The student is now practicing science, not following instructions. |

### 4.3 Core Methodological Terms

These terms are introduced across the first 8 modules (Phase 1) and reinforced continuously:

| Term | Introduced Via | Meaning Anchor |
|---|---|---|
| Hypothesis | Module 1 | The student's prediction before running the experiment |
| Independent variable | Module 2 | The one thing the student deliberately changes between trials |
| Dependent variable | Module 2 | The thing the student measures to see if something changed |
| Control | Module 3 | The trial where nothing is changed, used as a baseline for comparison |
| Observation | Module 1 | What the student sees, hears, or measures — distinct from interpretation |
| Data | Module 3 | The recorded measurements and observations, organized for analysis |
| Replication | Module 5 | Running the same experiment multiple times to verify consistency of results |
| Conclusion | Module 4 | A statement about whether the data supported or contradicted the hypothesis |
| Falsifiability | Module 6 | The principle that a hypothesis must be capable of being proven wrong to be scientific |
| Systematic error | Module 7 | A consistent flaw in the experimental setup that skews all results in the same direction |
| Peer review | Module 8 | The process of having another person evaluate your methods and conclusions |

## 5. Living Glossary

The student maintains a personal glossary throughout the curriculum. Unlike a textbook glossary, each entry is tied to a specific experiment the student performed.

### 5.1 Entry Format

> **OXIDATION**
>
> **Definition:** The loss of electrons by an atom, ion, or molecule during a chemical reaction.
>
> **First encountered:** Module 2 — "Can You Make a Battery from Fruit?"
>
> **I saw it when:** [Student writes their own observation, e.g., "The zinc strip got thinner and pitted after sitting in the lemon juice for 10 minutes."]
>
> **Connected to:** Reduction (opposite process), Electrochemistry, Redox reaction

### 5.2 Rules

- The student writes the "I saw it when" field themselves. This is non-negotiable. The act of translating observation into language is the mechanism of retention.
- Entries are added after each module, not in advance.
- The "Connected to" field is left blank initially and filled in as later modules introduce related terms. This creates a growing web of connections.
- The glossary can be a physical notebook, a digital document, or index cards. The medium does not matter; the act of writing does.

## 8. Research Report Process

Every module culminates in a mini research report. This is not a lab report. A lab report recites procedure and records data. A research report asks a question, uses an experiment to answer it, and argues for a conclusion. The student is an investigator writing for an audience, not a technician filling in blanks.

Over 62 modules, the student builds a portfolio of 62 research reports — a body of original scientific work, written in their own voice, using precise scientific language, grounded in experiments they conducted themselves.

### 8.1 Why Research Reports, Not Lab Reports

| Lab Report (avoided) | Research Report (used) |
|---|---|
| Recites the procedure as given | Describes the method the student chose and why |
| Records data in a table | Interprets data and argues for a conclusion |
| States a "purpose" | Poses a genuine research question |
| Passive voice, impersonal | First person, active voice — the student owns the investigation |
| Ends with "conclusion: the hypothesis was supported" | Ends with a new question the findings raised |
| Feels like homework | Feels like publishing your findings |

### 8.2 Report Structure

Each report follows six sections. These mirror the structure of a real scientific paper, scaled to be completable in 15–20 minutes of writing after each experiment.

| Section | Prompt to the Student | Scientific Skill Practiced | Length |
|---|---|---|---|
| **1. Research Question** | "What question are you trying to answer? Write it as a single, specific, testable question." | Formulating a precise, falsifiable question | 1–2 sentences |
| **2. Hypothesis** | "Based on what you already know, what do you predict will happen? State your prediction using the scientific terms you've learned, and explain your reasoning." | Making predictions grounded in prior knowledge; using precise terminology | 2–3 sentences |
| **3. Experimental Design** | "Describe what you did in your own words. What was your independent variable? Your dependent variable? What did you control? Why?" | Understanding variables, controls, and experimental logic | 1 short paragraph |
| **4. Results** | "What happened? Report your observations and data. Include numbers, measurements, descriptions. Sketches and tables encouraged. Do not interpret yet — just report." | Distinguishing observation from interpretation; accurate data recording | 1 paragraph + optional table/sketch |
| **5. Analysis and Conclusion** | "What do your results mean? Was your hypothesis supported or contradicted? Use specific data to support your argument. Connect your findings to this module's scientific concepts." | Evidence-based argumentation; connecting data to theory; vocabulary in context | 1–2 paragraphs |
| **6. Next Investigation** | "What new question did this experiment raise? If you could design a follow-up experiment, what would you test and why?" | Identifying gaps; generating new hypotheses; science as iterative | 2–4 sentences |

### 8.3 Scaffolding Progression for Reports

Just as the Method Sidebar (Section 4) gradually transfers ownership of the scientific method to the student, the research report process has its own scaffolding progression:

| Phase | Modules | Report Scaffolding |
|---|---|---|
| **1: Guided** | 1–15 | Each section includes a detailed prompt with an example of what a strong response looks like. Key scientific terms are suggested in the prompt. The report form highlights which terms from the module should appear in the analysis. |
| **2: Prompted** | 16–30 | Prompts remain but examples are removed. The student receives a checklist: "Did you state your independent variable? Did you use at least 2 terms from this module in your analysis? Did you cite specific data?" |
| **3: Structured** | 31–48 | Only section headings and one-sentence prompts remain. The student writes freely within the structure. The checklist becomes self-administered. |
| **4: Independent** | 49–62 | The student receives only the research question (or formulates their own in Phase 4 Designer modules). They produce the full report from the blank structure, mirroring the process of writing an actual research paper. |

### 8.4 Language Standards in Reports

The language policy (Section 3) applies with full force in the research reports. Specifically:

- All scientific terms introduced in the module must be used correctly in the report. The Analysis section is the primary location for demonstrating vocabulary mastery.
- Colloquial substitutions are flagged during review. If a student writes "the metal broke down," the feedback is: "What is the precise term for what happened to the zinc? Use it."
- Reports are written in first person, active voice: "I measured the voltage across the circuit" not "the voltage was measured." The student owns their investigation.
- The Hypothesis section must include reasoning, not just a prediction. "I think the lemon will produce more voltage" is incomplete. "I hypothesize that the lemon will produce a higher voltage than the potato because citric acid is a stronger electrolyte than starch, meaning more ions are available to facilitate the redox reaction" demonstrates understanding.

### 8.5 The Digital Report Tool

Students complete their research reports using an online form that walks them through each section with guided prompts. The form is not a blank page — it is structured to reduce the executive function overhead of "where do I start?" that is particularly challenging for ADHD learners.

The tool:

- **Presents one section at a time** to prevent overwhelm. The student sees only the current section and its prompt, not the entire report.
- **Includes the module's key terminology** as reference chips that the student can tap to see definitions. This keeps the glossary accessible without breaking flow.
- **Auto-saves continuously** so the student never loses work. They can leave and return without penalty.
- **Generates a formatted report** when complete. The output looks like a real mini research paper with the student's name, the module title, date, and all six sections formatted with proper headings.
- **Builds a portfolio over time.** All 62 reports accumulate into a single collection. By the end of the curriculum, the student has a tangible body of scientific work.

### 8.6 Review and Feedback

Reports are reviewed by the instructor (parent, tutor, or teacher) with a simple rubric focused on three dimensions:

| Dimension | What to Look For | Feedback Focus |
|---|---|---|
| **Vocabulary Precision** | Did the student use the module's scientific terms correctly? Are there colloquial substitutions? | Point to the specific term. Ask: "What is the scientific word for this?" |
| **Evidence-Based Reasoning** | Does the Analysis cite specific data from Results? Does the student connect observations to concepts? | Ask: "You said the hypothesis was supported — which measurement tells you that?" |
| **Investigative Thinking** | Does Next Investigation pose a genuine, testable question? Does it follow from the findings? | If the question is vague, ask: "What exactly would you change? What would you measure?" |

Feedback should be conversational, not corrective. The goal is to push the student toward greater precision, not to penalize incomplete thinking. A report that uses three out of five terms correctly is a success — the feedback targets the two that were missed or misused.

### 8.7 Portfolio and Progress Tracking

The 62 research reports collectively serve as both a learning artifact and an assessment tool:

- Early reports (Modules 1–15) will be shorter, rely more on prompts, and may have vocabulary gaps. This is expected.
- Mid-curriculum reports (Modules 16–40) should show increasing independence, longer analysis sections, and cross-references to earlier experiments.
- Late reports (Modules 41–62) should read as genuine mini research papers: self-directed questions, rigorous methodology, evidence-based conclusions, and sophisticated vocabulary.

Comparing Report 1 to Report 30 gives a concrete, visible measure of growth that the student can see for themselves. For an ADHD learner, this tangible evidence of progress is a powerful motivator.

### 8.8 AI Feedback System

The digital report tool (Section 8.5) includes an AI feedback layer that provides real-time coaching as the student writes each section of their research report. This system handles the draft-level feedback that would otherwise fall to the instructor, freeing the instructor to focus on portfolio-level review and deeper scientific conversation.

**Core Design Principles**

- **Questions only, never answers.** The AI asks one targeted question per feedback check. It never tells the student what to write, suggests sentences, or provides model text. Example: "What is the precise term for what happens when atoms lose electrons?" — not "You should use the word oxidation here."
- **No scores.** Each section displays a readiness signal only: green (solid), orange (has gaps), navy (unchecked). There is no numerical score, no percentage, no letter grade. The signal tells the student whether to revise or move on — nothing more.
- **One question at a time.** The AI never asks multiple questions or open-ended prompts. A single, specific question reduces cognitive load and gives the student a clear next action.
- **Optional to act on.** Every feedback message ends with: "Revise above if you'd like, or move on — this is your report." The student is never forced to revise. Autonomy is preserved.
- **Resource-aware hints.** The AI returns a hint field with each feedback response: "notebook" (the student needs their data), "terminology" (the student needs a precise term), or "none." When the hint is "terminology," the tool automatically opens the terminology reference panel so the student can scan for the right word without leaving their writing.

**Priority Hierarchy**

When the AI evaluates a section, it checks for issues in this order and surfaces only the highest-priority one:

1. Colloquial language where a precise scientific term exists (e.g., "the metal broke down" instead of "oxidation")
2. Claims without specific data (e.g., "the lemon was the best" without citing a voltage)
3. Missing reasoning or mechanism (e.g., a hypothesis with a prediction but no explanation)
4. Structural issues (e.g., interpreting results in the Results section instead of the Analysis)

**Feedback Persistence**

Feedback stays visible while the student revises. When the student changes their text significantly, the feedback dims to 55% opacity and displays: "You've revised — tap Check my work again when ready." Feedback never disappears on its own — the student always controls when to dismiss or refresh it.

**First-Draft Snapshots**

The tool automatically saves a snapshot of the student's first meaningful draft of each section. When the student reaches the preview screen, they can view a side-by-side comparison of their first draft and final version. This makes revision visible as a process, not a punishment.

**Cross-Domain Examples (Anti Copy-Paste)**

Within the digital tool, guidance examples come from other modules, not the current one. For instance, if the student is writing a C2 (fruit battery) report, the example research question comes from P1 (Forces and Motion), the example hypothesis from B3 (Photosynthesis), and so on. The student can see the pattern of strong scientific writing without being able to copy any content into their own report. Examples appear inside a collapsible guidance panel, framed as instructional context rather than model answers.

**Terminology Reference Panel**

A collapsible panel below the writing area displays the module's key terms in a three-column table: Term, Definition, and "You Observed It When..." The observation column is designed for scanning — the student reads their own experimental anchors to locate the right term. This panel auto-opens when the AI feedback hint is "terminology."

**Phase Progression**

The AI feedback system follows the same four-phase scaffolding model as the Method Sidebar (Section 4) and the report prompts (Section 8.3):

| Phase | Modules | AI Feedback Behavior |
|---|---|---|
| **1: Specific** | 1–15 | Concrete, targeted questions with resource hints. "What is the precise term for what happens when atoms lose electrons? Check the terminology panel — scan the 'You Observed It When' column." |
| **2: Broader** | 16–30 | Still asks questions, but resource hints are less specific. "Check your glossary" instead of pointing to a specific column. |
| **3: Open** | 31–48 | Asks the question with no resource pointer. The student locates their own resources. |
| **4: On Request** | 49–62 | AI feedback is available but not automatic. The student chooses when to request a check. In Designer modules, the student is expected to self-assess. |

**Paper Notebook → Digital Report Workflow**

The lab notebook and the research report are deliberately separate tools with separate media. The lab notebook is physical paper, used during the experiment for raw data capture. The research report is digital, written after the experiment using the notebook as source material. The entry screen of the digital tool reminds the student: "Have your lab notebook open to today's entry before you begin." This separation reinforces the distinction between data collection (observation, recording) and data analysis (interpretation, argumentation). Paper forces encoding at the moment of observation; the digital tool is for structured interpretation.

**Deployment**

The digital report tool is a Next.js application deployed on Vercel. The AI feedback layer uses Claude Sonnet via a server-side API route — the API key is stored as a Vercel environment variable and never reaches the browser. Cost per feedback check is approximately $0.003, yielding an estimated total cost of ~$2 for AI feedback across the full 62-module curriculum.

## 9. Lab Notebook

The student keeps a lab notebook throughout the curriculum. This is separate from both the glossary and the research reports. The notebook captures raw, in-the-moment experimental work — the rough notes taken during the experiment itself. The research report (Section 8) is the polished interpretation written afterward; the notebook is the raw material.

### 9.1 Notebook Entry Template

- Date and module number
- **My prediction:** (written before the experiment begins)
- **What I did:** (brief procedure notes in the student's own words)
- **What I observed:** (raw observations — numbers, sketches, descriptions)
- **What surprised me:** (specifically flagging counterintuitive results)
- **New terms I learned:** (list, with module reference)

### 9.2 Relationship Between Notebook and Report

The notebook is a working document used during the experiment. It is messy, informal, and immediate. The research report is written after the experiment is complete, using the notebook as source material. This two-step process — raw capture followed by structured interpretation — mirrors actual scientific practice and trains the student to distinguish between data collection and data analysis.

For ADHD learners, the act of writing by hand (if possible) during the experiment improves encoding and provides a physical anchor for the experience. The notebook should feel like a scratch pad, not an assignment.
