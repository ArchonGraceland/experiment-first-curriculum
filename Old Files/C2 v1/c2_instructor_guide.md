## Setup Checklist (Before the Student Arrives)

- **Lay out all materials on the work surface.** Having everything visible before the student starts reduces transition friction and keeps momentum.
- **Test one lemon cell yourself.** Confirm you get a reading on the multimeter (should be 0.7–1.0V). If the reading is zero, the zinc may be corroded — sand it lightly.
- **Pre-set the multimeter.** Set it to DC voltage (~2V range or auto-ranging). The student will switch to DC milliamps for Step 8.
- **Roll the lemon.** Give it a firm roll on the counter to break internal membranes. This dramatically improves results. Do this for all citrus fruits.
- **Have sandpaper accessible.** If the student reuses electrodes across fruits, the surface oxidizes and readings degrade. A quick sand restores them.
- **Lay newspaper or a tray under the workspace.** Fruit juice and metal fragments will get on the surface.
- **Have the lab notebook worksheet and pen ready at the workspace.** Not across the room. The lab notebook is a separate printed worksheet — the student fills it in by hand during the experiment.

---

## Safety Notes

> **This is a low-risk experiment.** The voltages are far below the threshold of human sensation (~1V vs. the ~50V needed to feel a tingle). The currents are in the microamp-to-milliamp range. There is no shock hazard.
>
> **However:**
>
> - **Goggles are required.** Not because this experiment is dangerous, but because goggles-as-habit is a safety practice you are building for later modules that do involve hazardous materials.
> - **Do not eat the fruit afterward.** Metal ions (especially zinc) have leached into the fruit. Discard all fruit used as electrolytes.
> - **Wash hands after handling zinc and copper strips.** Zinc dust and copper residue are mild irritants.
> - **If using sandpaper, sand away from the face and body.** Metal dust particles can irritate eyes.

## Expected Results Reference

The student module does not include expected values — this is deliberate, so observations feel like genuine discoveries. Use this section to confirm results are in the normal range and to diagnose problems.

**Single-cell voltages (zinc/copper electrodes):**

| Fruit | Typical Voltage | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Lemon | 0.8–1.0V | Highest and most consistent due to strong citric acid electrolyte |
| Orange | 0.8–0.9V | Similar to lemon; slightly lower acid concentration |
| Potato | 0.7–0.9V | Phosphoric acid electrolyte; can surprise students by being close to citrus |
| Apple | 0.6–0.8V | Malic acid; generally lowest of the four |

**Other expected readings:**

- **Two lemons in series:** ~1.6–1.8V (roughly double a single cell)
- **Circuit current with LED:** 0.2–1.5 mA (very small — explains dim LED)
- **Strips touching without fruit (negative control):** ~0V (confirms electrolyte is essential)
- **LED behavior:** Should glow dimly. If it doesn't light at all, see troubleshooting below.

**Electrode observations:**

- **Zinc strip (after sitting in lemon):** Pitting, roughening, or discoloration on the submerged portion. The exposed end should look clean by comparison. This is the visible evidence of oxidation.
- **Copper strip (inside lemon):** Tiny bubbles may form on the surface — hydrogen gas from reduction. These can be hard to see; not every student will observe them clearly, and that's fine.

**Challenge Mode expected results:**

- **Zinc + Iron:** Lower voltage than Zn/Cu (~0.3–0.5V). Iron is closer to zinc on the activity series.
- **Zinc + Zinc (same metal):** ~0V. No reactivity difference means no driving force.
- **Copper + Copper (same metal):** ~0V. Same reasoning.
- **Small non-zero reading on same-metal cells:** Normal. Caused by temperature differences between electrodes or surface oxide layers. This is a great teaching moment — ask "Why isn't it perfectly zero?"

**Theoretical reference:** Zinc standard electrode potential: −0.76V. Copper: +0.34V. Theoretical Zn/Cu cell voltage: 1.10V. Students measure less because fruit electrolytes are weak, electrodes are small, and internal resistance is high.

## Timing Guide

This breakdown assumes a 16-year-old with ADHD. The experiment is designed for short bursts of activity with immediate feedback.

| Time | Activity | Your Role |
|---|---|---|
| **0–2 min** | Read phenomenon hook, make prediction in notebook | Say nothing. Let them read and write. |
| **2–5 min** | Set up lemon cell (Step 1) | Only intervene if strips are touching inside the fruit. |
| **5–8 min** | Measure voltage (Step 2) | If multimeter shows nothing, check: correct setting? Probes on right strips? Fruit rolled? |
| **8–12 min** | Examine zinc, learn oxidation (Step 3) | Ask: "What does the zinc look like?" Do not supply the answer. |
| **12–15 min** | Learn reduction, examine copper (Step 4) | Ask: "If zinc is losing electrons, where do they go?" |
| **15–18 min** | Electrolyte test, negative control (Step 5) | This is the "aha" moment. Let them discover the zero reading. |
| **18–26 min** | Test 4 fruits, record data table (Step 6) | This is the longest stretch. If focus fades, say: "Which fruit do you think will beat the lemon?" |
| **26–32 min** | Wire series circuit, light LED (Step 7) | Help with wiring only if asked. The LED lighting is a strong motivator. |
| **32–35 min** | Measure current (Step 8) | Help switch multimeter to mA mode if needed. |
| **35–40 min** | Challenge Mode (optional but encouraged) | Ask: "What would happen with two of the same metal?" |
| **40–60 min** | Research report via digital tool | Available for terminology questions. Do not dictate. |

## How to Facilitate Without Lecturing

This is the hardest part of your job. The curriculum is designed so the student discovers every concept through observation. Your role is to create conditions for discovery, not to explain.

### The Golden Rules

> - **Never explain before they observe.** If the student asks "What is oxidation?" before Step 3, say: "Let's see what happens to the zinc first. Then we'll name it."
> - **Ask, don't tell.** Replace statements with questions. Instead of "The zinc is losing electrons," ask: "What happened to the zinc surface? What do you think is leaving the metal?"
> - **Let them struggle for 30 seconds before helping.** If the multimeter shows nothing, wait. They may figure out the probes are reversed or the setting is wrong. Productive struggle builds problem-solving skills.
> - **Celebrate the data, not the "right answer."** If their lemon produces 0.65V instead of 0.9V, that's still data. Ask: "Why do you think your reading is different? What could we change?"
> - **Never touch their notebook.** The notebook is theirs. Messy is fine. Sketches are encouraged. Crossed-out predictions are evidence of learning, not mistakes.

## Common Mistakes and Troubleshooting

| Problem | Likely Cause | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| **Multimeter reads 0V** | Probes reversed; multimeter on wrong setting (AC instead of DC); strips touching inside fruit; corroded electrodes | Check DC voltage setting. Swap red/black probes. Ensure strips don't touch. Sand electrode surfaces. |
| **Very low voltage (<0.5V)** | Fruit not rolled enough; electrodes too close together; small fruit with little juice | Roll fruit more aggressively. Space electrodes farther apart. Try a juicier fruit. |
| **LED doesn't light with 2 cells** | LED polarity reversed (long leg = positive); loose connections; cells wired in parallel instead of series | Check LED orientation. Check that copper of Cell 1 connects to zinc of Cell 2. Tighten all clips. |
| **Student mixes up oxidation and reduction** | Very common — these are the hardest terms in the module | Anchor to the physical: "Oxidation happens where the metal is dissolving. Which electrode dissolved? That's where oxidation happened." |
| **Student skips notebook entries** | Executive function challenge common with ADHD — the experiment is more engaging than writing | Build in micro-pauses: "Before we move to the next step, take 30 seconds to jot down what you just saw." |
| **Student rushes through without observing** | Hyperfocus on the endpoint (lighting the LED) rather than the process | Redirect: "The LED is coming — but first, look closely at this zinc strip. What happened to it?" |
| **Challenge Mode: same-metal cell reads small non-zero voltage** | Temperature differences between electrodes, or surface oxide layers creating a tiny galvanic potential | This is actually a great teaching moment. Ask: "Why isn't it perfectly zero? What's different between the two strips?" |

## What to Watch For

These are the signals that the module is working:

### Signs of Understanding

- The student uses "oxidation" and "reduction" correctly in conversation without prompting.
- The student predicts (before testing) that two same-metal electrodes will produce zero voltage.
- The student spontaneously connects the fruit battery to something else ("Is this how real batteries work?").
- The student's research report analysis section cites specific voltage readings.
- The student asks a new question at the end that is genuinely testable.

### Signs of Difficulty

- The student confuses oxidation and reduction consistently even after Step 4.
- The notebook has few or no entries — the writing barrier is too high.
- The student cannot explain why the voltage dropped to zero without the fruit.
- The research report's analysis uses none of the five key terms.

### If Difficulty Persists

Do not re-explain. Instead, go back to the bench. Have the student repeat Steps 3–5 with fresh electrodes, narrating aloud what they see. The physical act of observing the zinc pit again while saying "the zinc is losing electrons" is more effective than any explanation.

## Research Report Coaching Reference

The student module contains only the prompts for each report section — no examples, no model answers. This section gives you the examples and coaching tools to use conversationally if the student is stuck. **Do not hand these to the student or read them aloud as answers.** Use them to ask guiding questions.

### Section 1: Research Question — What a Strong One Looks Like

> *"Does the type of fruit used as an electrolyte affect the voltage produced in a galvanic cell with zinc and copper electrodes?"*

**What makes this strong:** It names both the independent variable (type of fruit) and the dependent variable (voltage), and specifies the constants (zinc and copper electrodes). If the student writes something vague like "Can fruit make electricity?" ask: "What exactly are you changing between trials? What are you measuring?"

### Section 2: Hypothesis — What a Strong One Looks Like

> *"I hypothesize that the lemon will produce a higher voltage than the potato because citric acid is a stronger electrolyte than the starch-based solution inside a potato, meaning more ions are available in the lemon to facilitate the redox reaction at the electrode surfaces."*

**What makes this strong:** The reasoning connects prediction to mechanism. "The lemon will work better" is a guess. "The lemon will produce higher voltage because its electrolyte has higher ion concentration" is a hypothesis. If the student's hypothesis has no reasoning, ask: "Why do you think that fruit will win? What's different about its juice?"

### Section 3: Experimental Design — What a Strong One Looks Like

> *"I tested four different fruits (lemon, potato, apple, orange) as electrolytes — that was my independent variable. My dependent variable was the voltage measured across the electrodes using a digital multimeter. I controlled the electrode materials (zinc and copper), the depth of insertion (~2.5 cm), and the distance between electrodes (~3 cm) to ensure any voltage differences were caused by the electrolyte, not by the setup."*

**If the student is stuck:** Ask: "What was the one thing you changed each time? What did you measure each time? What did you keep the same, and why?"

### Section 4: Results — What a Strong One Looks Like

> *"The lemon cell produced 0.92V. The orange cell produced 0.87V. The potato cell produced 0.83V. The apple cell produced 0.71V. Two lemon cells wired in series produced 1.78V and powered the LED at 0.4 mA. The zinc electrode in the lemon showed visible pitting after 15 minutes. Tiny bubbles were visible on the copper electrode."*

**Key coaching point:** Results report facts. Analysis interprets them. If the student starts explaining why in this section, say: "Save the why for the next section. Here, just tell me exactly what happened — numbers, units, what you saw."

### Section 5: Analysis & Conclusion — What a Strong One Looks Like

> *"My hypothesis was supported: the lemon produced the highest voltage (0.92V), consistent with citric acid being a stronger electrolyte. The pitting on the zinc electrode confirms that oxidation occurred — zinc atoms lost electrons to the external circuit. The bubbles on the copper electrode are evidence of reduction — hydrogen ions in the electrolyte gained electrons at the cathode. The voltage difference between fruits likely reflects differences in ion concentration and acid strength of each electrolyte."*

**This is the most important section.** The requirement is at least 4 of 5 key terms used correctly and at least 2 specific data points. If the student writes "the metal broke down," ask: "What's the precise scientific term for what happened to the zinc?" If they claim the hypothesis was supported but cite no numbers, ask: "Which measurement tells you that?"

### Section 6: Next Investigation — What a Strong One Looks Like

> *"I noticed the voltage slowly decreased over time. My next investigation would test whether electrolyte concentration affects voltage decay rate — specifically, I would dilute lemon juice to 75%, 50%, and 25% concentration, measure initial voltage for each, then record voltage every 5 minutes for 30 minutes. I predict that lower concentrations will show faster voltage decay because fewer ions are available to sustain the redox reaction."*

**If the student writes "I'd try more fruits":** Ask: "Which fruits? What would you measure? What do you predict would happen, and why?"

## Research Report Review Rubric

When reviewing the student's completed report, focus on three dimensions. Use conversational feedback, not corrections.

| Dimension | Strong | Needs Work |
|---|---|---|
| **Vocabulary Precision** | Uses 4–5 of the key terms correctly. "Oxidation at the zinc anode" is precise. | Uses colloquial substitutions: "the metal broke down" instead of "oxidation." Feedback: "What's the precise term for what happened to the zinc?" |
| **Evidence-Based Reasoning** | Cites specific data: "The lemon (0.92V) outperformed the potato (0.83V)." Connects data to the hypothesis. | Makes claims without data: "The lemon was the best." Feedback: "What voltage did it produce? How did that compare to the others?" |
| **Investigative Thinking** | Proposes a specific, testable next experiment with named variables and a prediction. | Vague next steps: "I'd try more fruits." Feedback: "Which fruits? What would you measure? What do you predict?" |

**Remember:** A Phase 1 report that uses 3 of 5 terms correctly is a success. The student is 16, has ADHD, and just encountered these terms for the first time 30 minutes ago. Calibrate your expectations accordingly. The goal is growth across 62 modules, not perfection on the first one.

## Content Knowledge Reference

You do not need to teach this material. This section exists so you can answer questions accurately if they arise.

**Why does fruit produce voltage?**

Fruit contains acidic solutions (citric acid in citrus, malic acid in apples, phosphoric acid in potatoes). These acids dissociate into ions in solution, creating an electrolyte. When two metals with different reactivities are placed in the electrolyte, the more reactive metal (zinc) spontaneously loses electrons (oxidation) while the less reactive metal (copper) facilitates the gain of electrons by ions in solution (reduction). The potential difference between the two half-reactions drives electron flow through the external circuit.

**Standard electrode potentials (for reference)**

Zinc: −0.76V. Copper: +0.34V. The theoretical voltage of a Zn/Cu cell is 0.34 − (−0.76) = 1.10V. The student will measure less than this because fruit electrolytes are weak, electrodes are small, and internal resistance is high. Typical measured values: 0.7–1.0V.

**Why does the same-metal cell read near-zero?**

With identical electrodes, there is no difference in reactivity, so no thermodynamic driving force for electron transfer. Any small reading is likely due to temperature differences between the two electrodes or surface oxide layers creating a minor galvanic potential.

**Connection to AP Chemistry**

This module introduces concepts that align with AP Chemistry Unit 4 (Chemical Reactions) and Unit 9 (Applications of Thermodynamics), specifically Learning Objectives ENE-6.A (galvanic cells) and TRA-2.A (oxidation-reduction). The student will encounter these formally in the AP-aligned benchmarks.

## Cleanup

- Discard all fruit — do not eat. Metal ions have leached into the flesh.
- Rinse and dry all zinc and copper strips. Sand lightly to remove oxidation if reusing.
- Wipe down the work surface — fruit juice corrodes metal surfaces.
- Store multimeter with probes disconnected to preserve battery.
- Wash hands.
